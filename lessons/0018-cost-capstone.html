Cost, Capacity, and the Senior Capstone

Every architecture decision in this course was secretly a cost decision. The final lesson makes the money explicit — $/million tokens, build vs buy, capacity planning — and ends with the artifact that closes the loop: a platform design doc you defend in a mock interview.

Knowledge

1. The unit economics of a token

$/1M output tokens ≈ (GPU $/hr × num_GPUs) / (throughput tok/s × 3600) × 1e6

H100 @ ~$2.5/hr, 70B fp8 TP=2 doing ~2500 tok/s:
  (2.5 × 2) / (2500 × 3600) × 1e6  ≈  $0.56 / 1M output tokens — at 100% utilization

The killer variable is utilization. At 20% average load your real cost is 5× the napkin number — and 20–40% is TYPICAL for diurnal chat traffic. Everything you built exists to push that number up: continuous batching (lesson 3), routing (6), autoscaling (10), bin-packing (11), disaggregation (16).

3. Build vs buy, without religion

API cost/mo  = tokens/mo × $/token           (scales with usage, zero floor)
Self-host/mo = GPUs × $/hr × 730 + eng_time  (scales with PEAK capacity, high floor)

Breakeven usually lands at sustained hundreds of millions of tokens/month — but the decision is rarely pure math: data governance (prompts leave your VPC?), latency control, model customization (LoRA fleets), and rate-limit sovereignty all push build; spiky/low volume and zero infra headcount push buy. The senior answer is a table, not a slogan — and "start on APIs, instrument usage, revisit at the breakeven curve" is usually the honest recommendation.

4. Capacity planning as a repeatable procedure

1. Forecast: peak RPS, tokens in/out distribution, growth, tenant mix
2. Measure:  bench ONE replica on target hardware (lesson 9 — you know how)
3. Divide:   replicas = peak_demand / per-replica goodput (at SLO, not at max!)
4. Headroom: ×1.3–1.5 for N-1 failures, deploys, cache warmup (lesson 10's lesson)
5. Re-run:   every model change, quantization change, workload drift

Per-replica goodput at SLO — the knee from your lesson-9 curve, not the peak — is the number that goes in the spreadsheet. You've already measured one; most engineers never have.

5. The design doc is the senior deliverable

Working structure: Context & requirements → SLOs → Architecture (+ the two designs you rejected and why) → Capacity & cost model → Failure modes & blast radii → Security/tenancy → Rollout & operations → Open questions. If lessons 11–17 each left you an artifact, this doc mostly assembles them — that was the plan all along.

Check yourself

Napkin says $0.56/1M tokens; the bill implies $2.80. Most likely gap?

5× gap ≈ 20% utilization — the classic diurnal-traffic number. The napkin assumed the GPUs never idle; they mostly do. Egress on text is pennies.

Capacity plan divides demand by which per-replica number?

Beyond the knee you serve tokens that violate the SLO — capacity that "exists" but can't be sold. Planning on saturation throughput bakes in SLO breaches at peak.

Strongest single argument for self-hosting despite API convenience?

Governance is binary and non-negotiable for some buyers; cost flips with volume, and API vendors do host fine-tunes. When data-residency is the requirement, the math becomes secondary.
