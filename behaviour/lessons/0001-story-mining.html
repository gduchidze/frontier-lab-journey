Lesson 0001 · Phase A — Raw Material

Story Mining

Before any technique: dig up the true events. You cannot polish stories you haven't found.

Mission tie. Every behavioral question at all three labs is answered from the same bank of 8–10 real stories. Anthropic's culture round additionally demands live reasoning — but even there, reasoning needs real events to reason about. This lesson builds the raw material everything else refines.

1 · Why mining comes first

The reported failure pattern at senior level is not bad delivery — it is thin material: candidates reach for their two favorite war stories, repeat them across rounds, and have nothing when the interviewer asks for a failure or a values conflict. The fix is an inventory pass over your whole recent career before any question practice. (Senior-engineer behavioral guide)

One more research finding to internalize now: at Anthropic's culture round, rehearsed STAR stories are an explicit failure mode — interviewers are trained to detect performance and probe past it. Bullets, not scripts, from day one. (Preper Anthropic guide)

2 · The mining protocol (do this now — 30 minutes)

Open a blank note. Scan the last 3–4 years, job by job, project by project. For each trigger below, write every event that surfaces — one line each, no filtering, aim for 15+ candidates:

  1. An incident or outage where you were in the room. What did you decide?
  2. A time you disagreed with your manager or a senior engineer. Out loud.
  3. Something you shipped that failed — visibly, with your name attached.
  4. A project with no clear owner or spec that you picked up anyway.
  5. A time you changed a team's process, tooling, or architecture — and it stuck.
  6. A deadline you refused, or a corner you refused to cut. What did it cost you?
  7. A time you were flat-out wrong and someone junior to you was right.
  8. Cross-team work where the other side had opposite incentives.
  9. The hardest feedback you ever received. What changed after?
  10. A moment your personal ethics and the business's interest pointed different ways.

Triggers 3, 7, and 10 feel worst to write. Those are the highest-value entries — failure (C3) and values-under-pressure (C6) are the competencies frontier labs weight hardest and candidates prep least.

3 · Select and outline

From your 15+, pick the 8–10 with real stakes and a measurable result. For each, write the 6-bullet outline from the Story Bank Matrix template — context, stakes, 2–3 actions, metric result, one honest learned-sentence. Then fill the matrix grid: every story gets checked against the six competencies; every competency column needs ≥2 stories.

Senior bar check For each Result bullet ask: did anything about how the organization works change? "Fixed the bug" is a mid-level story. "We now canary every model rollout because of this" is a senior one. If no story clears this bar yet, that's lesson 0002's job — mine anyway.

4 · Project step

Deliverable before next session: the filled matrix (hand-written or in a note) + 6-bullet outlines for your top 8. Bring the two emptiest competency columns to your teacher — that gap drives lesson 0002. Then try one drill: say "drill me — Anthropic" and answer whatever comes, out loud, from bullets only.
