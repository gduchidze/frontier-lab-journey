Reliability Patterns

Systems rarely die from the failure itself — they die from their own defenses. Timeouts, retries, breakers, bulkheads, and shedding, plus the ways each one, misused, becomes the outage.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design prompts always reach the same follow-up: "and what happens when that dependency slows down?" Candidates who say "add retries" have just designed a retry storm; candidates who name the timeout budget, the retry budget, the breaker thresholds, and the shed policy — with numbers — are describing a system that survives its own Tuesday. This lesson is the vocabulary of Release It! (Nygard) and the AWS Builders' Library, with the classic incidents (payments brownouts, flash sales) where each pattern earned its name. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · Anatomy of a cascading failure

Reliability is a design discipline because the default behavior of a distributed system under partial failure is to convert one slow dependency into a total outage. The canonical sequence, straight out of Release It!: your checkout service calls a payments provider whose p99 quietly goes from 200 ms to 10 s. Nothing "fails" — every call eventually returns. But by Little's law, at 100 RPS × 10 s latency you now hold 1,000 concurrent requests against a thread pool of 200. The pool fills in seconds; requests that never touch payments queue behind the ones that do; the whole service — browse, search, cart — is now as slow as its slowest dependency. Latency is contagious.

Two consequences frame everything below. First, slow is worse than down: a dead dependency fails fast and frees your threads; a slow one holds them hostage. Second, the defenses interact — a retry is extra load, a fallback is untested code, a queue is deferred latency — so each pattern here has a failure-modes section of its own (§9). Google's SRE book calls the end state a cascading failure: overload → server death → more overload on the survivors; AWS goes further and argues you should distrust even your recovery paths (Avoiding fallback in distributed systems).

2 · Timeouts — every remote call has one

The non-negotiable rule: every remote call has a timeout, always — a call without one is a thread you have donated to someone else's incident (AWS Builders' Library: Timeouts, retries and backoff with jitter). Two numbers, not one: a connect timeout (short — establishing a TCP connection to a healthy peer is fast, so a few hundred ms is generous) and a request timeout (how long you will wait for the answer). Pick the request timeout from the dependency's measured p99 plus headroom — not from vibes. Set it near the p50 and you time out healthy requests; set it at 30 s "to be safe" and you have rebuilt the hostage scenario of §1.

Timeout budgets. Timeouts must decompose down the call chain: each hop's timeout fits inside what its caller has left, or the deeper timeout is decorative — the caller gives up first and the "protection" protects nobody.

HopTimeoutWhy this number
Client → edge2,000 msThe product promise: checkout answers in 2 s or apologizes
Edge → checkout service1,500 msLeaves the edge 500 ms for one retry or a clean error page
Checkout → payments provider800 msProvider p99 is 250 ms; 3× headroom, still inside the budget
Checkout → inventory DB300 msp99 is 40 ms; anything slower is already an anomaly

Say the invariant out loud in an interview: child timeout < parent's remaining budget, at every level. A 5 s timeout below a 2 s timeout is a bug you can spot in an architecture diagram.

3 · Retries done right

A retry is a bet that the failure was transient — and it is extra load offered to a system that just told you it is struggling. Three rules make the bet safe (Builders' Library):

  • Only retry idempotent operations, and only transient errors. Retrying a non-idempotent payment capture is how customers get charged twice (lesson 0014's idempotency keys exist for exactly this). A 400 will fail identically forever; don't retry it.
  • Exponential backoff with full jitter. Plain exponential backoff (100, 200, 400 ms…) keeps clients that failed together retrying together — synchronized waves hammering the recovering service at exactly the same instants. Full jitter — sleep a random time in [0, min(cap, base·2ⁿ)] — decorrelates them. The AWS Architecture Blog's simulation of contending clients found full jitter roughly halves total calls versus plain exponential backoff while finishing the work sooner: same retries, spread instead of stacked. You will reproduce the spread-vs-stack effect in the lab (E3).
  • Retry budgets. Cap retries as a fraction of base traffic — e.g. retries ≤ 10% of requests. When the budget is spent, fail instead of retrying: a burst of failures then costs at most 1.1× load, not a multiple of it.

Worked example — the arithmetic interviewers want to hear. Retries compound multiplicatively across layers. Edge retries 3×, service retries 3×, data layer retries 3×: during a full outage of the bottom dependency, one client request becomes up to 3 × 3 × 3 = 27 attempts at the bottom. At 1,000 RPS of client demand, the dying dependency receives 27,000 attempts/s — a 27× self-inflicted DDoS, delivered at the worst possible moment, and it persists until the retries drain. In the lab, even 2 layers × 3 attempts at only 50% failure pushes offered load to 3.3× — and the storm outlives the failure window by 3 seconds, because the dependency has to dig out from under the backlog the retries created. The fixes stack: retry at one layer (usually the edge), budget it, and put a circuit breaker in front so retrying stops entirely when the dependency is truly down.

4 · Circuit breakers — fail fast, probe gently

A circuit breaker wraps calls to a dependency and tracks recent outcomes; when the failure rate crosses a threshold, it trips and subsequent calls fail immediately without touching the dependency (Fowler: CircuitBreaker; the pattern was canonized in Release It!). The state machine you must be able to draw:

StateBehavior on each callTransitions when
Closed (normal)Pass the call through; record success/failure in a rolling windowFailure rate ≥ threshold (e.g. 50% over 20 calls) → Open
Open (tripped)Reject instantly — no network, fail in microsecondsCooldown elapses (e.g. 2 s) → Half-open
Half-open (probing)Let a limited probe through; reject the restProbe succeeds → Closed; probe fails → Open

What fail-fast buys. Without a breaker, every call during an outage eats the full timeout: in the lab, 10 s of outage at 50 calls/s with a 1 s timeout costs the caller 500 caller-seconds of blocked thread time — the §1 cascade in miniature. With the breaker, one window of calls pays the timeout, the trip converts every later failure to microseconds (lab: 14 caller-seconds total, a 36× reduction), and — just as important — the dependency gets silence in which to recover instead of a timeout-paced battering. The half-open probe is the elegance: the system discovers recovery by spending one request, not a stampede.

5 · Bulkheads — partition so damage stays local

Nygard named this after ship compartments: one flooded compartment must not sink the vessel. In software: give each dependency (or tenant) its own bounded pool — threads, connections, semaphore permits — so the §1 scenario becomes "the payments pool of 30 threads is exhausted; browse and search, running on their own pools, don't notice." The math of §1 reverses: 100 RPS × 10 s still wants 1,000 threads, but it can only ever take the 30 you allocated it. Payments degrade; the site survives. The same partitioning applies per-tenant: one customer sending pathological traffic exhausts their pool, not yours.

Zoom out and the bulkhead becomes architecture. Cell-based architecture partitions the entire stack into independent cells, each serving a slice of customers, so a poison request or bad deploy takes out one cell, not the service. Shuffle sharding refines it: assign each customer a random small subset of nodes, and two customers are unlikely to share their full subset — a noisy customer degrades a combination, and almost nobody else shares that exact combination (AWS Builders' Library: Workload isolation using shuffle sharding). This is how AWS keeps one poisoned Route 53 customer from being everyone's outage. Blast radius is a design input, not a post-incident metric.

6 · Load shedding & graceful degradation

When offered load exceeds capacity, you will fail to serve someone. The only choice is who and how. The wrong answer is implicit: queue everything, let latency climb until every response arrives after its caller stopped waiting — 100% effort, 0% goodput. The right answer is explicit: reject at admission, cheaply and early, with a 503 and Retry-After, keeping accepted requests fast (AWS Builders' Library: Using load shedding to avoid overload; SRE book: Handling overload). Recall the utilization–latency curve from lesson 0001: past ~80–90% utilization, waiting time goes vertical — admission control at ~0.9C is how you refuse to climb the cliff. In the lab, a ramp to 2× capacity gives the unshed queue a p99 of 5.8 s and growing; admission control rejects 816 requests and holds survivors flat.

Not all requests are equal — shed by priority:

ClassExamplesUnder overload
CriticalCheckout, payment capture, health checksShed last — dropping health checks makes the LB remove healthy servers and worsen the overload
StandardProduct pages, search, cart readsShed after degradables; serve stale cache where possible
DegradableRecommendations, analytics beacons, prefetchShed first — or turn the feature off entirely

Graceful degradation is shedding features instead of users: during a flash sale, drop recommendations, serve yesterday's bestseller list from cache, and keep checkout pristine. A browned-out site that sells things beats a beautiful error page. Brownout > blackout.

7 · Backpressure recap — bounded queues everywhere

Shedding is backpressure at the front door; the same law applies to every queue inside the system. An unbounded queue is a latency bomb: it converts overload into unbounded delay, hides the problem from producers, and — when the consumer restarts — greets it with hours of backlog of mostly-expired work (AWS Builders' Library: Avoiding insurmountable queue backlogs). You measured this in lesson 0008's lab: bound every queue, and decide at enqueue time what happens when it is full — block the producer, drop oldest, or reject. "The queue will absorb it" is only true until the queue is the outage.

8 · Chaos engineering — rehearse the failure

Every pattern above is code that runs only on a bad day — which means, untested, it runs for the first time in production during an incident. Chaos engineering is the discipline of running that code on purpose: form a hypothesis ("if the payments provider times out, checkout degrades to async capture and p99 stays under 2 s"), inject the fault with a controlled blast radius (one cell, 1% of traffic, business hours, hand on the abort switch), and compare against steady state (principlesofchaos.org). The lineage is Netflix's Chaos Monkey, which randomly kills production instances precisely so that surviving instance death is a daily non-event instead of an annual crisis (Netflix Tech Blog: The Simian Army). The socialized version is the game day: a scheduled exercise where the team injects a scripted failure and practices the response — testing the humans and runbooks, not just the code.

9 · Failure modes — when the defenses attack

The lesson's thesis, cashed out. Each pattern misused is an outage pattern:

  • The flapping breaker. A too-small sample window (5 calls) or hair-trigger threshold makes the breaker trip on noise, reclose on a lucky probe, and trip again — callers see errors arriving in confusing bursts and the dependency never gets clean recovery silence. You will produce a flap in the lab (E2). Size the window for statistical significance; make the cooldown generous.
  • The globally shared retry budget. One retry budget for all tenants means one bad tenant's failures burn the entire budget, and now nobody gets retries — a noisy-neighbor attack on your own defense. Budgets need bulkheads too: per-tenant, per-dependency.
  • The fallback that fails on the day. The fallback path runs rarely, so it is untested, under-provisioned, and drifting — and it is invoked exactly when the system is already degraded. That is the core of AWS's argument for avoiding fallback: prefer defenses that are always exercised (shedding, throttling, static stability) over a second code path that must work, cold, during a fire.
  • The decorative timeout. A downstream timeout longer than the caller's remaining budget (§2) protects nothing: the caller has already hung up. Audit the chain end-to-end.
  • Shedding the cheap requests. A shedder that drops by arrival order or at random sheds $0 analytics beacons and $400 checkouts with equal enthusiasm — or worse, sheds health checks, so the load balancer amputates healthy servers mid-overload. Priority is the whole point of shedding (§6).

10 · One AI note, then back to fundamentals

Everything above transfers to GPU serving with the prices raised: a GPU worker's "thread pool" is its batch queue, so admission control sits in front of the queue (reject or route away when queue depth × service time exceeds the latency SLO — the §6 math with seconds-long service times); per-tenant bulkheads keep one customer's 100k-token jobs from starving everyone's chat traffic; and the brownout move is degrading to a smaller model — under overload, route standard-tier requests to the cheap model and keep the flagship for premium traffic. Same patterns, pricier misses. That is the whole note; §1–§9 are what the interview is grading.

11 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson live, in order:

  1. Open with the cascade (§1): "my first concern is a slow dependency exhausting the caller's concurrency — slow is worse than down." This frames everything else as deliberate.
  2. Timeouts with a budget (§2): state the edge promise (2 s) and decompose it per hop. Child < parent's remainder, always.
  3. Retries with three qualifiers (§3): idempotent only, backoff with full jitter, budget ≤ 10% — then the 27× multiplication story for why retries live at one layer.
  4. Breaker with numbers (§4): "50% failures over 20 calls trips it; 2 s cooldown; half-open probe recloses." Naming thresholds is what separates the pattern from the buzzword.
  5. Bulkheads and blast radius (§5): per-dependency pools first, then "at platform scale I'd cell-partition and shuffle-shard tenants."
  6. Shed by priority, degrade features (§6): 503 + Retry-After at 0.9C, health checks and checkout protected, recommendations dropped first. Brownout > blackout.
  7. Close with verification (§8): "none of this counts until a game day has exercised it — untested fallback is the outage."

13 · Project step

Write the reliability policy for gateway→worker calls in the LLM inference platform: timeout, retry budget, breaker thresholds, shed policy — and defend it in two sentences. A defensible pick: request timeout = 1.2× the p99 decode time for the requested max-tokens, one retry only for idempotent-safe failures under a 10% per-tenant retry budget, breaker at 50% failures over 20 calls with a 5 s cooldown per worker, and admission control ahead of the batch queue that sheds free-tier traffic first with 503 + Retry-After. Write your own — all four numbers, two sentences of defense — and bring it to the next session for critique.
