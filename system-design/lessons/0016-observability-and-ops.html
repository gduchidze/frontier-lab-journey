Observability & Ops

You can't fix what you can't see. Logs, metrics, and traces; SLOs and error budgets; and alerting that pages a human only for human-worthy problems.

Mission tie. Every Anthropic design prompt ends the same way: "how do you know it's working — and how do you know when it isn't?" Candidates who answer "add monitoring" stall; candidates who name three SLIs, a burn-rate page, and what the on-call does at 3 a.m. sound like they have carried a pager. This lesson is a compression of the Google SRE book — the industry's canonical answer — into the vocabulary you deploy live, using an e-commerce checkout and a payments API as the running examples.

1 · Three pillars, honestly

Three telemetry signals, three different questions, three very different bills (SRE book ch. 6, OpenTelemetry signals):

SignalWhat it isQuestion it answersCost profile
LogsDiscrete events, arbitrary detail"What exactly happened to request X?"Scales with traffic × verbosity — the expensive one
MetricsPre-aggregated numbers over time"Is checkout healthy right now? Trending how?"Scales with cardinality (distinct label sets), not traffic
TracesRequest-scoped causality across services"Which hop made this request slow?"Scales with traffic — hence sampling

The cardinality trap, up front: a metric's storage cost is the number of distinct label combinations, because each combination is its own time series. checkout_requests_total{service, status} is a handful of series. Add a user_id label and you mint one series per user — a million users is a million series, and your metrics bill now scales with your user base. Rule: labels are for bounded dimensions (service, status, region); unbounded identity (user, order, trace) belongs in logs and traces (Prometheus: do not overuse labels). You will detonate this bomb safely in the lab.

2 · Structured logging

Logs are for machines first, humans second. That means JSON, not prose: {"ts":…, "level":"error", "service":"payments", "request_id":"r-8f3a", "msg":"card_declined", "latency_ms":212}. Three disciplines make the difference (12-Factor: logs as event streams):

  • Correlation IDs everywhere. The gateway mints a request ID and every hop — orders, payments, retries, queue consumers — carries it. One grep reconstructs one request's story across twelve services; without it, logs are twelve unrelated diaries.
  • Levels used honestly. ERROR means a human should eventually look; WARN means degraded-but-handled; INFO is the narrative; DEBUG is off in production. A codebase where everything is ERROR has no ERROR level.
  • Sampling for volume. Keep every error log; sample the happy path. The errors are where the information is.

Worked cost — the arithmetic interviewers want to hear. A checkout flow at 1,000 RPS emitting ~2 KB of logs per request = 2 MB/s = 173 GB/day ≈ 5.2 TB per 30-day month, raw. Index and replicate 3× and you are storing ~15 TB; at managed-vendor ingest pricing of roughly $0.50/GB that is ~$2,600/month before you query anything. Sample INFO at 1:10 (keeping all WARN/ERROR) and the bill drops ~80% while incidents stay fully debuggable. Logging is a product decision with a unit cost, not free exhaust.

3 · Metrics that matter: RED vs USE

Two checklists cover nearly every dashboard you will ever need. RED is for services (request-driven things); USE is for resources (things with capacity) (Tom Wilkie: the RED method, Brendan Gregg: the USE method):

RED — per serviceUSE — per resource
LettersRate, Errors, DurationUtilization, Saturation, Errors
Applies toGateway, orders, payments — anything with requestsCPU, memory, disk, connection pools, queues, GPUs
Asks"Are users being served, correctly, quickly?""Is this component full, backed up, or failing?"
AudienceThe on-call at 3 a.m. — symptomsCapacity planning and diagnosis — causes

Percentiles, not averages — recap from Phase 1: a 30 ms average checkout latency is compatible with a p99 of 2 s, and at 30 object-fetches per page nearly every user experiences the tail. Report p50/p95/p99. Since you cannot average percentiles across hosts, production systems record histograms — counters per latency bucket — which aggregate correctly and let you compute any quantile server-side (Prometheus: histograms and summaries). Choose bucket boundaries around your SLO threshold (e.g. one bucket edge exactly at 400 ms) so "fraction of requests under SLO" is exact, not interpolated.

4 · Distributed tracing

A trace is one request's tree: each hop records a span (name, start, duration, parent) and the whole tree shares a trace ID. Context rides along in the traceparent HTTP header — the W3C Trace Context standard — so gateway, orders, and payments all stamp the same ID without coordination. The design descends from Google's Dapper paper; OpenTelemetry is its open standardization.

What traces uniquely find: metrics tell you checkout p99 jumped to 900 ms; only the trace shows the 12-service fan-out where 835 of those milliseconds sit inside the payments hop. Aggregates say when and what; traces say where and why, for this request.

Sampling, because tracing everything at 100k RPS is unaffordable: head sampling decides at trace start (cheap, simple — but blind: it drops interesting traces as readily as boring ones), while tail sampling decides after the outcome is known (keep all errors and slow traces, 1% of the rest) at the cost of buffering every trace until it completes (OpenTelemetry: sampling). The classic failure: 1:1000 head sampling means the one trace you need during the incident was probably discarded — the lab's E4 makes you feel this.

5 · SLI, SLO, error budget

The SRE book's central machinery (Service Level Objectives):

  • SLI — a service level indicator, best expressed as a ratio: good events / total events. Checkout availability SLI = non-5xx responses / all responses. Latency SLI = responses under 400 ms / all responses.
  • SLO — the target on that ratio over a window: "99.9% of checkout requests succeed, measured over 30 days."
  • Error budget — the complement: 1 − SLO. It reframes reliability as a resource you deliberately spend — on deploys, experiments, and risk — rather than a purity contest (Embracing Risk).

Worked math. A 30-day month is 43,200 minutes. A 99.9% SLO leaves a budget of 0.1% → 43.2 minutes of full outage (or the equivalent spread thin: 0.1% of requests failing continuously). At 99.95% the budget halves to 21.6 min; at 99.99% it is 4.3 min — less than one bad deploy plus one slow rollback, which is why every extra nine multiplies cost. In request terms: at 201,600 requests/month, a 99.9% SLO allows ~202 bad requests — and in the lab you will watch one 11-minute error burst spend 72% of that budget.

Budget policy is what gives the number teeth: while budget remains, ship fast; when the budget is spent, feature releases freeze and engineering shifts to reliability until the window recovers. Agreed in advance between product and engineering, so the 3 a.m. argument was already settled at noon. And keep the terms straight: an SLO is an internal target that pages engineers; an SLA is an external contract that pages lawyers — your SLO must be stricter than your SLA.

6 · Alerting philosophy: page on burn rate

Rule one, from SRE book ch. 6: page on symptoms, not causes. "CPU at 95%" is a cause — maybe harmless, maybe not; it becomes a ticket. "Users are failing checkout fast enough to threaten the SLO" is a symptom — it pages. Every page must be actionable: if the responder's first thought is "so what?", the alert is miscalibrated and is training humans to ignore pages.

The modern mechanism is the burn rate: how fast you are consuming error budget, as a multiple of the sustainable rate. Burn 1× = spending exactly the budget over the window; burn 14.4× = the whole 30-day budget gone in 50 hours. Worked example (SRE workbook: Alerting on SLOs): with a 99.9% SLO, an hour at 1.44% error rate is a 14.4× burn and spends 1/720 × 14.4 = 2% of the monthly budget in one hour — page-worthy. The workbook's recommended shape is multi-window, multi-burn-rate: page when burn ≥ 14.4× over both 1 h and 5 min (the short window makes the alert reset promptly); open a ticket when burn ≥ 6× over 6 h + 30 min (slow leaks that erode the budget without drama). Compare with the naive alternative — "page if error rate > 2% in any minute" — which pages for 60-second self-healing blips, and, because it watches raw errors instead of the SLI, never notices a latency-only incident at all. The lab scores exactly this contest.

7 · Incident response

When the page fires, process beats heroics (SRE book: Managing Incidents). One person becomes Incident Commander — coordinating, not typing; separate hands do Ops (mitigation) and Comms (stakeholder updates on a fixed cadence, e.g. every 30 min, so nobody interrupts the responders to ask "any update?"). Severity levels decide the machinery:

SevExample (payments API)Response
SEV-1Checkout down / money moving wronglyPage now; IC assigned; comms cadence; all hands as needed
SEV-2Degraded: elevated declines, one region slowPage on-call; mitigate; may escalate
SEV-3Budget-eroding slow leak, no user pain yetTicket; business-hours fix

MTTR anatomy: time-to-recover = detect → diagnose → mitigate. Observability compresses the first two (burn-rate alert detects; RED dashboard + trace diagnose); Lesson 0015's patterns compress the third (rollback, failover, feature flag off). Mitigate first, root-cause later — the incident ends when users stop hurting, not when you understand why.

Afterwards, the blameless postmortem (Postmortem Culture): timeline, contributing causes framed as system gaps rather than human failings — people who fear blame hide the details you need — and, crucially, action items with owners and due dates. A postmortem without tracked action items is a confession, not an improvement.

8 · Ops maturity: the boring superpowers

What separates teams that survive their own growth:

  • Runbooks. Every page links to a doc: what this alert means, first three checks, mitigation steps, escalation path. Written when calm, executed when panicking.
  • One dashboard per service, RED across the top row, dependencies below. The on-call should reach the right graph in two clicks, not scroll through forty vanity boards.
  • Deploy observability. Deploys cause most incidents, so make them observable: canary a new version to a slice and diff its RED metrics against the baseline before promoting — automated rollback if error or latency deltas exceed a bound.
  • Toil budget. Cap manual, repetitive ops work (~50% in Google's practice) and spend the rest engineering it away — else operations consumes the team that runs it (Eliminating Toil).
  • Error-budget-driven release gating. The CI/CD pipeline reads the budget: budget healthy → deploys flow; budget spent → only reliability fixes ship. Policy enforced by machinery, not by arguments.

9 · The failure modes that decide interviews

Observability itself fails, and interviewers probe for the scars (SRE book ch. 6):

  • Alert fatigue. Dozens of non-actionable pages a week train the on-call to swipe them away — and the real SEV-1 page gets swiped with them. Every false page you tolerate is a tax on the true one. Fix: delete or demote every alert that did not lead to action; measure pages-per-shift.
  • Dashboard sprawl. Forty dashboards nobody opens is negative value: during an incident, choice paralysis. Fix: one per service, RED top row, everything else earns its place or dies.
  • Unbounded cardinality. The user_id label from §1 — the bill arrives as a six-figure metrics invoice or an OOM'd Prometheus. Fix: lint label sets in code review; cardinality budgets per team.
  • Tracing sampled to uselessness. 1:1000 head sampling saves money all year, then the one trace that explains the outage was dropped. Fix: tail-based sampling — keep 100% of errors and slow traces, sample only the boring majority.
  • Postmortems without follow-through. A beautiful document, zero completed action items, same outage next quarter. Fix: action items live in the sprint board with owners; review completion monthly.
  • Nobody monitors the monitoring. The alert pipeline dies silently and the silence reads as health. Fix: a dead-man's switch — an alert that fires continuously by design (Alertmanager's Watchdog); a downstream system pages when it stops arriving (Prometheus alerting overview).

10 · One AI note, then back to fundamentals

Model serving changes the SLI menu, not the machinery. For the inference platform: TTFT p99 (time to first token — the user-perceived latency), tokens/s per request (streaming throughput), and queue depth per tenant (the saturation signal that predicts the next minute's pain) are your RED-side SLIs; GPU utilization and KV-cache occupancy are USE territory — dashboards and capacity, not pages. One discipline matters: eval-quality metrics are not ops metrics. "Is the model giving good answers" is measured by offline eval pipelines on their own cadence; "is the service up and fast" is measured by the SLIs above — mixing the pipelines gets you paged for a benchmark regression at 3 a.m. (OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions). Same SLO math, same burn-rate pages, new indicator names.

11 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson live, in order:

  1. Open with SLIs, unprompted. "Success criteria: availability SLI = non-5xx/total, latency SLI = under-400ms/total, both at 99.9% over 30 days." Instant credibility.
  2. Say the budget number. "99.9% monthly is 43.2 minutes of budget — that bounds how aggressive our deploys can be."
  3. Name the three signals with jobs (§1): metrics for detection, traces for localization, logs for the request-level story — joined by a correlation ID.
  4. RED per service, USE per resource (§3), histograms not averages, bucket edge at the SLO threshold.
  5. Page on burn rate, not thresholds (§6): "14.4× over 1h+5m pages; 6× over 6h tickets; CPU alerts are tickets, never pages."
  6. Sketch the incident path (§7): page → IC → mitigate (rollback/flag) → blameless postmortem with owned action items. Mention MTTR = detect + diagnose + mitigate.
  7. Volunteer a failure mode (§9): "the risk in my design is cardinality — I'd lint label sets and keep identity in traces." Raising it first is the strong-hire move.

12 · Lab — page yourself, correctly

Hands-on (do this before the quiz) Everything above is now verifiable in labs/lab-0016-observability-lab/: python3 observability_lab.py replays a compressed "month" through a gateway → orders → payments chain with two injected faults, then runs three experiments — RED tables that expose both faults in numbers, SLO math showing one 6-minute window eating ~78% of the monthly latency budget, and burn-rate paging beating a naive threshold on false pages and incident coverage — plus a span-tree reconstruction that names the guilty hop. 13 · Project step

Decision time for the LLM inference gateway: define its three SLIs + SLOs and one burn-rate page, and defend the package in two sentences. A defensible pick: availability (non-5xx/total, 99.9%/30d), TTFT p99 < 1.5 s (as a good-event ratio, 99%/30d), and per-tenant queue depth as the saturation dashboard signal — with one page: burn ≥ 14.4× over 1h+5m on the availability SLI, everything else a ticket. Two sentences: users feel failures and first-token delay, so those two page; queue depth predicts them but predicts imperfectly, so it informs capacity instead of waking anyone. Write your own three SLIs + one page and bring them to the next session for critique.
