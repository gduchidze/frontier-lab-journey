The Interview OS: the 4-Step Framework

Phase 1 gave you replication, sharding, caching, and queues. This lesson is the operating system that schedules them under a 50-minute clock.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design round hands you one open-ended sentence and then watches who drives. The interviewer is a collaborator you consult — not a guide you wait for. Candidates who need prompting to move from requirements to estimates to design fail the bar regardless of how much they know: the round is explicitly calibrated for engineers who structure ambiguity themselves. This lesson turns the Phase-1 toolkit into a repeatable execution loop. (Exponent's Anthropic guide)

1 · The 50-minute shape: you drive

The prompt will be one sentence — "design a package-tracking system for a logistics company", "design the checkout flow for a global e-commerce site", "design a news feed." Everything after that sentence is yours to structure. Silence reads as being lost; an agenda read aloud reads as seniority. So your literal first sentence should be the agenda: "I'll spend a few minutes on requirements, do quick capacity math, sketch the high-level design, then go deep on whatever looks hardest — interrupt me anywhere." That last clause matters: it invites collaboration on your terms instead of waiting for rescue.

StepBudgetOutput on the whiteboardFailure smell
1 · Clarify5–8 minWritten requirements + explicit out-of-scope listDesigning a product nobody asked for
2 · Estimate~5 minQPS, storage, bandwidth — numbers you will reuseNumbers computed, never referenced again
3 · High-level design~15 minBoxes and arrows, API sketch, data model sketchBoxes with no narrated trade-offs
4 · Deep dive + scale~20 min1–2 components under load; bottlenecks, failures, monitoringBreadth everywhere, depth nowhere

This is the structure every serious guide converges on — the Primer's four steps and interviewing.io's senior guide use the same skeleton, and interviewing.io's data-backed observation is blunt: strong candidates are distinguished less by what they know than by how they allocate the hour. The budgets are guardrails, not laws; the skill is noticing when you're over and saying so out loud ("I'm spending too long here — parking this, moving to the data model").

2 · Step 1 — Clarify (5–8 min): buy the right problem

Open-ended prompts are deliberately underspecified. "Design checkout" for a boutique with 100 orders/day and for Amazon on Prime Day are different systems with different correct answers. Step 1 is where you buy the right problem, and you buy it with four question families:

  • Users & use cases. Who calls this and why? Shoppers and the fraud team hit checkout very differently; drivers, warehouses, and customers hit package tracking differently.
  • Scale. Daily active users, requests/day, data volume, growth. "Are we at 1M or 100M users?" is a one-second question that changes every downstream decision.
  • Read/write ratio. A social feed is ~100:1 reads; a payments ledger is write-heavy and audit-read. The ratio picks your caching and storage strategy before you draw anything.
  • SLOs. Latency target at which percentile, availability target, and — critically for payments — consistency: is a double-charge acceptable? (No.) Is a feed that's 30 seconds stale acceptable? (Yes.)

Then do two things weak candidates skip. First, write the answers down where both of you can see them — they are the grading contract for the rest of the hour, and referring back to them later ("we said 99.9%, so this single point of failure is disqualifying") is one of the cheapest senior signals available. Second, separate the two requirement types explicitly:

TypeQuestion it answersCheckout example
FunctionalWhat does the system do?Add to cart, apply discount, charge card exactly once, email receipt
Non-functionalHow well must it do it?p99 < 500 ms at 30k QPS peak, 99.99% availability, no double-charges ever

Finally, state what is out of scope: "I'll assume auth, fraud scoring, and tax calculation are existing services I can call." Scoping is not dodging — it is what senior engineers do to real projects every week, and it protects your clock. Write the exclusions down too, so a late "but what about fraud?" gets the answer "scoped out at minute 3, happy to pull it back in."

3 · Step 2 — Estimate (~5 min): numbers you will spend later

Run the lesson-0002 sequence — users → QPS (with a peak multiplier) → storage → bandwidth, powers of ten only, narrated aloud (Primer: back-of-the-envelope, and your own Estimation Kit). Worked example for a social feed prompt:

200M DAU, each opens the feed 10×/day
  → 2B feed reads/day ÷ ~100k s/day ≈ 20k QPS average, ×3 peak ≈ 60k QPS
Writes: 100M new posts/day ≈ 1.2k posts/s
  → read/write ratio ≈ 50:1  →  this is a READ problem
Storage: 100M posts/day × 1 KB ≈ 100 GB/day ≈ 36 TB/year (before media)

The trap — named by every guide and by interviewers themselves — is computing numbers and never touching them again. Every estimate must be spent on a decision:

  • "60k QPS peak means one box won't do → load balancer + N stateless replicas."
  • "50:1 read-heavy → cache the rendered feed; fan-out-on-write becomes viable."
  • "36 TB/year won't fit one Postgres by year two → partition by user ID from day one."

A useful physical habit: draw an arrow next to each number as you use it. At the end of the interview, un-arrowed numbers are wasted minutes — and interviewers notice the difference between candidates who estimate as ritual and candidates who estimate as input.

4 · Step 3 — High-level design (~15 min): boxes, API, data model

Now, and only now, draw. Three artifacts, in order:

Boxes and arrows. Client → CDN/edge → load balancer → stateless services → cache → storage → async workers behind a queue. Follow one request through the whole path out loud — "a shopper hits place order, the order service writes the order as PENDING, drops a message on the queue, and returns; payment capture and email happen asynchronously." Tracing one concrete request is the fastest way to expose a missing arrow.

API sketch. 3–5 endpoints with verbs is plenty; resist designing all forty. For checkout:

POST /cart/{id}/items          add item
POST /orders                   create order from cart (Idempotency-Key header)
GET  /orders/{id}              order status
POST /payments                 capture payment (Idempotency-Key header)

That Idempotency-Key header is a whole senior signal in one line: retries happen, networks fail after the charge succeeds, and exactly-once effect requires the client to name the operation so the server can deduplicate — the pattern Stripe's API is built around (Stripe: designing robust and predictable APIs with idempotency).

Data model sketch. Entities, keys, and which store each lives in. orders(order_id PK, user_id, status, total) in a relational store because order state transitions want transactions; sessions in Redis because they're ephemeral and hot; events append-only in a log store because audit wants immutability. You are not designing the schema — you are proving you know that different data has different homes.

Through all of it, narrate trade-offs while drawing: "I'm putting a queue between order placement and email so a slow mail provider can't block checkout — the cost is the receipt arriving seconds later, which our SLOs don't care about." A diagram with narrated costs is a design; a diagram without them is clip art.

5 · Step 4 — Deep dive + scale (~20 min): where offers are won

The largest budget goes here because this is where senior signal lives. Pick the 1–2 components where the hard problems are — or, better, offer the choice: "I could go deep on making payment capture exactly-once, or on scaling feed fan-out for celebrity accounts; do you have a preference?" Either answer wins: you have shown you know where both dragons live.

Then apply the Phase-1 toolkit deliberately, each tool justified by a Step-2 number or a Step-1 SLO:

ToolReach for it when…Classic worked example
ReplicationAvailability SLO forbids single points of failure; reads outgrow one nodeOrder DB: 1 primary + 2 replicas; reads of order history go to replicas
ShardingWrites or data volume exceed one node36 TB/year of posts → shard by user_id; watch for celebrity hot shards
CachingRead-heavy ratio + tolerance for stalenessProduct pages at 50:1 reads → cache-aside with TTL; invalidate on price change
QueuesSpiky producers, slow consumers, or work that may retryFlash sale: absorb the 100× spike in a queue; workers drain at sustainable rate

Two things must come from you unprompted — interviewers deliberately wait to see if they do:

  • The bottleneck. "At 60k QPS the feed cache is fine, but the fan-out workers are the bottleneck when an account with 50M followers posts — that write amplification is why Twitter's timeline design fans out on write for normal users and falls back to fan-out-on-read for celebrities." (Primer: Twitter timeline design)
  • A failure mode, with mitigation. "If the payment provider times out after charging, we must not retry blindly — that's the double-charge. The idempotency key plus a reconciliation job closes it." Naming the failure before the interviewer's inevitable "what if X dies?" is worth more than a perfect answer after it.

Close the hour on your terms, not the clock's: monitoring ("the metric that pages a human is p99 checkout latency and payment error rate, not CPU") and the more-time list ("with more time I'd address multi-region failover and the analytics pipeline"). That closing sentence converts everything you didn't cover from an omission into a roadmap.

6 · Worked miniature: the heartbeat problem

Here is the full loop compressed, on the exact problem your lab uses — monitor 100,000 servers, alert within 30 s of one going silent (a logistics/fleet-infrastructure staple):

  1. Clarify. Functional: register server, receive heartbeats, alert on silence, ack alerts. Non-functional: detection p99 < 30 s, 99.9% availability, false-alarm rate low enough that on-call trusts it. Out of scope: dashboards, auth, billing.
  2. Estimate. 10 s heartbeat interval → 10k heartbeats/s; 100-byte payload → ~1 MB/s in — trivial bandwidth, non-trivial write rate. Storing every heartbeat = ~86 GB/day of nearly useless rows → spend that number: keep only last_seen state (100k rows, fits in memory) plus counters.
  3. Design. POST /heartbeat, GET /servers/{id}/status, POST /alert-rules. Ingest tier (stateless, behind LB) updates an in-memory last_seen store; a detector sweeps for now − last_seen > 30 s; alerts go through a queue so a slow pager integration can't block detection.
  4. Deep dive. Bottleneck: the sweep at 100k entries is fine, but the last_seen store is a SPOF → replicate it, or shard detectors by server-ID range. Failure mode named unprompted: who watches the watcher? A dead detector makes its whole partition go silent — so detectors heartbeat each other. Second failure mode: a network partition makes 20k healthy servers "go silent" at once → add alert coalescing ("1 datacenter down", not 20k pages). Monitoring: alert-pipeline lag is the metric that pages a human.

Notice the shape: every design choice traced back to a requirement or a number, and the two failure modes arrived before anyone asked. That shape is the interview.

8 · Communication moves: the three verbal tools

Three moves carry the whole hour, and all three are scripts you can rehearse:

  • Signposting. "For feed delivery I see three options: fan-out-on-write, fan-out-on-read, or a hybrid. I'd pick the hybrid because our 50:1 read ratio rewards precomputation but celebrity accounts make pure fan-out-on-write explode." Enumerate → choose → because. It proves you saw the design space and can still commit.
  • Checking in. "Want me to go deeper on the reconciliation job, or move on to scaling?" Once per major section is the right frequency — it steers you toward what the interviewer wants scored, and it converts them from examiner into collaborator, which is exactly the dynamic Anthropic's round is designed to test for.
  • Thinking aloud. Narrate forks even when you resolve them fast: "SQL or NoSQL for orders… state transitions want transactions, so SQL." Ten words, and a decision that would have been invisible becomes a scored data point.

One warning: communication moves are amplifiers, not substitutes. Signposting three bad options is still three bad options — the moves exist to make the Phase-1 knowledge you actually have visible.

9 · If the prompt is an AI system

At Anthropic the prompt may be AI-flavored — "design a model-serving gateway" — but the framework does not change, and that is the point. Clarify still asks users/scale/SLOs (the SLOs become TTFT and tokens/sec — Glossary); Estimate still converts users to QPS to hardware count; the deep dive still reaches for replication, sharding, caching, and queues, just wearing GPU-shaped clothes (batching queues, KV-cache). Phases 3–4 supply that domain content. If you can execute the loop on checkout and heartbeats, you can execute it on inference — the OS is the transferable asset.

10 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson live — lines that carry disproportionate signal:

  • Opening: "Let me spend five minutes on requirements before any boxes — I want to make sure I'm designing the right system." (Announces the framework; buys silence-free structure.)
  • Leaving Step 1: "So the contract is: 60k QPS peak, p99 under 500 ms, 99.99% availability, and fraud is out of scope — correct?" (Confirmation converts your notes into an agreed rubric.)
  • Spending a number: "We said 50:1 reads — that number is why I'm adding a cache here and not a bigger database."
  • Offering the deep dive: "The two hard problems are exactly-once payment and hot-shard fan-out. I can go deep on either — preference?"
  • Unprompted failure mode: "Before you ask what breaks: the payment-provider timeout after a successful charge is the scary one. Here's how the idempotency key closes it."
  • Closing: "To operate this I'd page on p99 latency and error rate. With more time: multi-region failover and the reconciliation audit job." (Ends the hour as the driver, not as the clock's victim.)

11 · Lab — your first full rep

Hands-on (do this before the quiz) The framework only becomes yours under a clock. In labs/lab-0009-design-rep/, python3 design_rep.py runs a timed 4-step rep against a real prompt (a heartbeat service watching 100,000 servers — section 6's problem, now yours to solve cold), tracks your elapsed time per step, then walks you through a 12-dimension self-scoring rubric and saves everything to rep-notes.txt. Speak your answers aloud — type only bullet notes. Run python3 design_rep.py --demo first to preview the structure and rubric.

12 · Project step

Do one full timed rep with design_rep.py — out loud, alone in a room, no pausing the clock. Score yourself honestly on all 12 rubric dimensions and bring the generated rep-notes.txt to the next session; your teacher will critique the scores, not the design. A rep where you ran out of time in Step 3 is a better deliverable than no rep — the whole point of Phase 1's closing lesson is to find out where your clock breaks before an interviewer does.
