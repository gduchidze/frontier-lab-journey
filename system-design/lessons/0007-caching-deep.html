Caching, Deeply

Why a box of memory beats a database, where to put it, how to keep it honest — and the three failure modes that decide whether your interview answer survives.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design prompts — "design a token-generation service at 100,000 RPS" — are unaffordable without caching, and the follow-up is always the same: "what happens when that cache entry expires under load?" Candidates who say "add Redis" stall; candidates who name the update pattern, the eviction policy, the staleness bound, and the stampede fix keep going. This lesson gives you those names, with the classic systems (e-commerce catalogs, social feeds, payments) where each one earned its reputation. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · Why caching works: physics × skew

Two facts multiply together, and every caching decision traces back to one of them.

Fact one — physics. A main-memory reference is ~100 ns; a round trip inside a datacenter is ~500 µs — the 5,000× cliff from the Estimation Kit. Add a disk-backed database lookup behind that hop and you are comparing sub-millisecond in-memory reads against single-digit-millisecond (or worse) queries. Serving from memory instead of another hop-plus-disk is not an optimization; it is a different regime.

Fact two — skew. Real traffic is Zipf-distributed: the k-th most popular key gets roughly 1/ks of the traffic, so a tiny fraction of keys receives most requests. Amazon sells millions of products, but today's front-page items dominate reads; Twitter hosts hundreds of millions of accounts, but a few thousand celebrities dominate timeline fan-in. That is why a cache holding 0.2% of the keyspace can absorb nearly half of all reads — you will measure exactly this in the lab. Caching works because traffic is unfair; on uniform traffic a small cache is nearly useless.

Worked example — the arithmetic interviewers want to hear. An e-commerce product page fans out to ~30 object reads (product, price, stock, reviews, seller…). At 5,000 pages/s that is 150,000 reads/s. A well-tuned relational primary is comfortable around 10,000 reads/s — so the cache tier must absorb ≥ 93% of reads before the database is even viable. And the latency win compounds: with hit rate h, effective read latency ≈ h × 0.5 ms + (1 − h) × 8 ms. At h = 0.9 that is 1.25 ms; at h = 0.99 it is 0.575 ms — and the DB sees 10× fewer reads at the first and 100× fewer at the second. Hit rate is simultaneously a latency number and a capacity number.

2 · Where caches live, top to bottom

Every layer of the stack caches; in an interview, walk the ladder before choosing your layer (Primer §Cache):

LayerWhat it cachesYou control it viaTypical win
BrowserStatic assets, API responsesHTTP cache headers (RFC 9111)Zero network at all
CDNStatic + cacheable dynamic content, at the edgeTTLs, cache keys, purge API (Cloudflare: what is a CDN)Ocean crossings avoided (~150 ms each)
Reverse proxyWhole responses at your own edgeNginx / Varnish configApp servers never touched
App-layer cacheObjects, query results, sessions, computed aggregatesRedis / Memcached — your design spaceDB reads avoided
Database internalPages, query plansBuffer pool sizing — mostly automaticDisk reads avoided

Rules of thumb for the ladder: cache as high as the content's shareability allows (a logo is CDN material; "your cart" is not), and remember each layer has its own invalidation story — a CDN purge API and a Redis DEL are different systems that fail differently. Design interviews live almost entirely at the app layer: what do you key on, how does the cache learn about writes, and what gets evicted. The canonical large-scale reference is Facebook, which runs Memcached as a massive "look-aside" (cache-aside) tier in front of MySQL and treats it as core infrastructure, not an add-on (Scaling Memcache at Facebook, NSDI '13).

3 · What to cache, and what the key is

Three granularities, coarse to fine — each with a classic home:

  • Full page / response caching. Anonymous content that renders the same for everyone: news article pages, product listings for logged-out users. Highest leverage, most fragile to personalization — one "Hello, Giorgi" in the corner ruins it (hence edge tricks like caching the page and filling the user fragment client-side).
  • Query / aggregate caching. The result of an expensive computation keyed by its inputs: "top-10 trending posts", "average rating for product 4711". Watch out: keying on raw query strings is brittle — normalize inputs (sorted params, canonical casing) or identical questions become distinct cache entries.
  • Object caching. One domain object per key: user:8123, product:4711, session:abc. The workhorse. Fine-grained keys invalidate precisely (a price change touches one entry, not every page containing it) at the cost of more round trips per screen — which batched multi-gets (MGET) claw back.

Interview habit: whenever you say "I'll cache that," immediately say the key and the value out loud. "Key: product:{id}:v2, value: the serialized product JSON, ~2 KB." Vague caches cannot be invalidated; named ones can. (The :v2 is a version prefix — bumping it is the cheapest whole-cache invalidation there is.)

4 · The four update patterns

The heart of the topic: when data changes, how do cache and database stay related? Four patterns, each a different answer (Primer §When to update the cache):

PatternRead/write pathWinsPaysClassic home
Cache-aside (lazy / look-aside)App checks cache; on miss reads DB, fills cacheOnly requested data cached; cache failure ≠ outageMiss costs 3 trips; staleness window after writesProduct catalogs; Facebook's memcache tier
Write-throughEvery write goes to cache and DB synchronouslyCache never stale vs DB; reads always warm after writesSlower writes; cold cache until first write; caches unread dataUser profiles; DynamoDB DAX
Write-behind (write-back)Write to cache, ack immediately; flush to DB asynchronously, often batchedFast writes; absorbs write bursts; batches DB I/OData loss if cache dies before flush; complex recoveryLike/view counters; social-metrics pipelines
Refresh-aheadCache re-fetches hot entries before expiryHot keys never expire in a user's face; smooths DB loadWasted — extra load, even — when prediction is wrongHomepages, leaderboards, exchange rates

Cache-aside in slow motion (know this cold): read → miss → read DB → write cache → return. Three trips on a miss, one on a hit. On writes the app updates the DB and then invalidates (deletes) the cache entry rather than updating it — deleting sidesteps the race where two concurrent writes update the cache in the wrong order and strand a stale value indefinitely. Even so, a narrow race remains (read miss reads old DB value, write lands, reader fills cache with the old value); Facebook closes it with leases — more in §6.

Worked numbers. Mixed workload, 90% reads / 10% writes, hot keys. Cache-aside invalidation means every write evicts a hot key, and the next reader re-buys it from the DB: you pay staleness protection with extra misses. Write-through keeps those keys warm — in the lab this exact setup shows write-through winning read hit rate ~75% vs ~68% with identical DB write counts. Flip the ratio to write-heavy and write-through's discipline becomes a tax on keys nobody reads. There is no globally best pattern; there is a best pattern per read/write mix — say the mix first, then the pattern.

Payments footnote. Write-behind is the pattern to treat with fear in anything money-shaped: a cache node dying with unflushed ledger writes is data loss, full stop. Payment systems either skip caching writes entirely or pair the cache with a durable log so the "behind" part is replayable. Counters of likes can shrug; balances cannot.

5 · Eviction and sizing

A full cache must throw something out. The three policies you must be able to contrast:

PolicyEvictsStrong againstWeak against
LRULeast-recently-usedTemporal locality (what was just read will be read again)Scans — one bulk export flushes the whole working set
LFULeast-frequently-usedScan pollution; stable popularityShifting popularity — slow to forget yesterday's hot keys
TTLWhatever has expiredUnbounded staleness — it is a freshness bound, not a space policyChoosing the number (too short: misses; too long: staleness)

In practice you compose them: TTL for freshness plus LRU/LFU for space. Production systems approximate: Redis samples a handful of keys and evicts the best candidate rather than maintaining a perfect global order — precision isn't worth the bookkeeping at that scale (Redis eviction docs, which offer both allkeys-lru and allkeys-lfu).

Sizing follows the hit-rate curve. Because of Zipf skew, the first slots buy huge hit-rate gains and each subsequent multiplication of size buys less. In the lab: 100 slots (0.2% of keys) → ~47% hit rate; 100× more memory → only ~86%. Size at the knee of the curve, then sanity-check memory: 20 M hot products × 2 KB ≈ 40 GB — a handful of cache nodes, entirely reasonable; 2 B keys × 2 KB = 4 TB — now you are sharding a cache cluster and should ask whether you actually need the cold 99%. Justify any size with the 5,000× cliff: every point of hit rate is that many requests moved across it.

6 · Invalidation, honestly

"There are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things" (Phil Karlton, canonized in every caching talk since). The reason it is hard: an invalidation is a message about a write, delivered to a different system, racing the reads. You have exactly two honest strategies:

  • Event-driven invalidation. Writes publish "key X changed" — via CDC on the database's replication log, or application-level pub/sub — and consumers delete or update cache entries. Precise and fast, but the invalidation pipeline is now itself a distributed system that can lag, reorder, or drop; Facebook drives memcache deletes from the MySQL commit stream precisely so an invalidation can never be lost ahead of the write it describes (NSDI '13, §4). Their leases mechanism additionally fixes the stale-set race: a miss returns a token, and a fill is accepted only if no invalidation arrived in between.
  • TTL-and-tolerate. Entries expire on a clock; between write and expiry, readers may see the old value — and you state that bound out loud: "prices may be up to 60 s stale; checkout re-reads the DB." Simple, no pipeline to operate, self-healing. Saying the bound is what makes this engineering rather than hoping.

Most real systems mix them: TTL as the backstop, events for the keys where staleness is expensive. And note the e-commerce trick in the example above — tolerate staleness on the browse path, bypass the cache on the money path. Consistency budgets differ within one product.

7 · The failure modes that decide interviews

Adding a cache adds three new ways to fall over. Interviewers probe these because they separate "has used Redis" from "has been paged about Redis."

  • Cache stampede (thundering herd / dogpile). A hot key expires; every in-flight request misses simultaneously and every one recomputes from the DB. Do the math out loud: a key serving 1,000 RPS whose recompute takes 200 ms accumulates ~200 concurrent identical DB queries the instant it expires — a self-inflicted 200× spike, at peak traffic, on your hottest data, possibly slowing the DB so the herd grows further. Three fixes, in escalating sophistication:
    1. Per-key locking ("singleflight"): one request recomputes; the rest wait for its result. Instagram implements this as promises — the first miss stores a promise in the cache and the herd awaits it instead of the backend (Instagram: Thundering Herds & Promises). Facebook's leases have the same effect (NSDI '13).
    2. TTL jitter: randomize expiries so many hot keys never expire in the same instant. This is the fix for the fleet-wide variant — cache avalanche, where a deploy or restart sets thousands of keys to the same TTL and they all expire together. Jitter does not reduce total reloads; it decorrelates them in time.
    3. Refresh-ahead: renew the entry before users can ever observe the expiry (§4). Strongest for the keys you can predict — which is exactly its limit.
  • Cache penetration. Requests for keys that don't exist miss forever and hammer the DB every time — accidentally (deleted product IDs in old links) or adversarially (scanning random IDs). Fixes: negative caching — cache the "not found" itself with a short TTL — and, for hostile or vast keyspaces, a Bloom filter in front that answers "definitely not present" without touching anything.
  • Hot-key overload. One celebrity key exceeds what a single cache node can serve — the flash-sale item, the viral post. A cache shard handles ~100k ops/s; a viral key wanting 1 M ops/s needs more than one home. Fixes: replicate the key (append a random suffix from a small set — post:99:{0..9} — and fan reads across the copies), or add a tiny in-process cache in each app server for the top-N keys. Netflix's EVCache leans on replication across nodes and zones for exactly this class of problem (Netflix Tech Blog: EVCache).

8 · Scaling the cache tier itself

Once the cache outgrows one node, it becomes a distributed system with the same three questions as any other. Partitioning: spread keys across nodes with consistent hashing so adding a node remaps ~1/N of keys instead of all of them (you met the ring in earlier lessons; Memcached clients have shipped it since 2007). Replication: copies for read fan-out and for surviving node loss — losing a cache node is "only" a hit-rate crater, but at Facebook scale a cold cluster would flatten MySQL, which is why cluster warm-up and cross-region invalidation streams get their own sections in the memcache paper. Failure containment: when the cache tier degrades, the DB inherits the full read load exactly when things are worst — so cap DB concurrency (connection pools, load shedding) so a cache incident degrades latency rather than cascading into a full outage. A cache is a shock absorber; make sure removing it bends the system rather than breaking it.

9 · One AI note, then back to fundamentals

Everything above transfers unchanged to model serving: an inference gateway response cache is cache-aside with TTL-and-tolerate; a prefix/KV cache is object caching where the "object" is attention state and the system prompt is the ultimate hot key; and when a popular prompt's cached response expires, the dogpile math of §7 applies with GPU-sized recompute costs — which is why singleflight sits in front of inference. Same patterns, pricier misses. That is the whole note; the fundamentals in §1–§8 are what the interview is actually grading.

10 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson live, in order:

  1. Motivate with numbers, not vibes. "Reads are 150k/s against a DB good for 10k/s, so I need ≥ 93% of reads served from cache" beats "let's add caching for performance."
  2. Walk the ladder once (§2), then commit: "static assets to the CDN; the design problem is the app-layer cache."
  3. Name key and value. "Key product:{id}:v2, value ~2 KB JSON" — this unlocks concrete sizing and invalidation talk.
  4. State the read/write mix, then pick the pattern (§4) — cache-aside by default, and say why you're not using the others: "write-behind is off the table; this touches balances."
  5. Give the staleness bound out loud (§6): "60 s TTL on the browse path; checkout bypasses the cache." Unprompted staleness bounds are a strong-hire signal.
  6. Raise the failure modes before the interviewer does (§7): "the risk here is stampede on the flash-sale key — I'll put singleflight in front of recompute and jitter the TTLs." Then eviction (§5) and what happens when the cache tier dies (§8).
  7. Sizing sanity check to close: entries × bytes vs the hit-rate knee — "40 GB covers the hot set; past that we're buying single points of hit rate with doublings of memory."

A defensible pick: cache-aside with TTL-and-tolerate (responses keyed on a normalized prompt hash, 5-minute TTL, staleness stated) plus per-key singleflight and TTL jitter so an expiring popular entry never dogpiles the backends.
