Streams & Events

The log as system-of-record — Kafka's model, event-driven architecture, event sourcing/CQRS, and what "exactly-once" really means when a payment email must go out once and only once.

Mission tie. Every Anthropic-scale design prompt eventually sprouts an arrow labeled "events" — "how do downstream services learn an order shipped?", "how do you meter usage for billing?" — and the follow-ups are always the same: "what ordering do you actually get?" and "what happens when a consumer crashes mid-batch?" Candidates who say "put it on Kafka" stall; candidates who name the partition key, the delivery semantics, and the idempotency plan keep going. The backbone here is DDIA chapter 11 plus Jay Kreps' "The Log" — the essay that explains why LinkedIn built Kafka at all.

1 · Message queue vs event log

Classic message brokers (RabbitMQ, SQS) treat a message as a task: the broker hands it to one consumer, the consumer acks, the broker deletes it (RabbitMQ: acknowledgements). Kafka treats a message as a fact: an immutable record appended to a retained log; consumers just remember how far they've read (an offset), and reading destroys nothing (Kafka design docs).

That one difference — delete-on-ack vs retain-and-track — changes everything downstream. With a retained log you can: attach a second consumer (analytics) to the same order stream without touching the first (fulfillment); replay last week's events through a fixed bug; rewind a broken consumer to before its bad deploy; and bootstrap a brand-new service from history. Kreps' insight in "The Log" was that LinkedIn's dozens of point-to-point pipelines (activity events → warehouse, → search index, → recommendations…) were all secretly the same thing — log shipping — and one durable, ordered, replayable log could replace the O(N²) mess of custom integrations. DDIA ch11 makes the same move formally: a stream is a log; consumers are just cursors.

Rule of thumb for interviews: queue when work should happen once, soon, by whoever's free (resize this image); log when a fact has multiple audiences over time (an order was placed — fulfillment, email, fraud, analytics all care, some of them next year).

2 · Kafka's model, precisely

Four nouns, and the whole system falls out of them (kafka.apache.org design):

ConceptWhat it isWhat it buys you
TopicA named category of events (orders, clicks)Namespacing; per-topic retention and schema
PartitionAn append-only, totally-ordered log; a topic is P of themThe unit of ordering AND of parallelism
OffsetA consumer's position in one partition, stored by the consumer groupCheap resume, rewind, replay — no per-message broker state
Consumer groupA set of consumers sharing work; each partition is owned by exactly one memberHorizontal scaling with per-partition ordering intact

The partition is where ordering and parallelism trade off: events within one partition are totally ordered; events across partitions are not ordered at all. Consumers scale by claiming partitions — and when a consumer joins or dies, the group rebalances, reassigning partition ownership. So the maximum useful consumer count in a group equals the partition count: consumer #9 on an 8-partition topic owns nothing and does nothing (the lab shows exactly this).

Worked example — the arithmetic interviewers want to hear. A checkout clickstream produces 200,000 events/s at ~1 KB each = 200 MB/s. A partition comfortably sustains ~10 MB/s of writes, so the broker side needs ≥ 20 partitions. But your consumers enrich each event and write to a store at ~2,500 events/s each, so the consumer side needs 200,000 / 2,500 = 80 consumers — and since consumers ≤ partitions, you need ≥ 80 partitions. The consumer side, not the broker side, usually sets the partition count. Provision ~96 for headroom, and say out loud that repartitioning later reshuffles which key lands where — which is why you overprovision now.

3 · The partition key decides what "ordered" means

The producer picks a partition by hashing a key: all events with the same key land in the same partition, in send order. So Kafka's real ordering guarantee is per key, and choosing the key is choosing your ordering contract:

  • order_id: every event of one order (created → paid → shipped) is ordered — exactly what an order pipeline needs. Different orders interleave freely, which is fine.
  • user_id: one user's actions are ordered (needed for sessionization, fraud rules) — but a bot or a celebrity account becomes a hot key, and its whole partition becomes the group's slowest lane. Same celebrity problem as sharded databases and hot cache keys — third appearance in this course, same fix instincts.
  • Random / no key: perfectly even partitions, maximum throughput, zero ordering. Fine for independent facts (page views for counting); disastrous for anything stateful.

In the lab, zipf(1.1) keys make the hottest key carry 15.9% of all traffic, pinning one partition to 23% of events — 1.87× its fair share — and that partition alone caps the whole consumer group's finish time. Say the key and the skew story together: "keyed by order_id; order IDs are uniform so no hot partition" is a complete sentence; "keyed by user_id" without a skew plan is a trap you set for yourself.

4 · Delivery semantics: where duplicates actually come from

GuaranteeMechanismFailure costUse when
At-most-onceCommit offset before processingCrash mid-batch → events silently lostMetrics, telemetry — losing a few is fine
At-least-onceCommit offset after processingCrash before commit → batch redelivered, processed twiceDefault for anything that matters + idempotent consumer
Exactly-onceIdempotent producer + transactions (Kafka-internal), or at-least-once + dedupComplexity; covers Kafka→Kafka, not external side effectsStream processing pipelines (Kafka Streams)

Duplicates enter at two doors. Producer side: a send times out, the producer retries, but the first attempt had actually landed — now the log holds the event twice. Kafka's idempotent producer closes this door with per-producer sequence numbers (Kafka docs §4.6, message delivery semantics). Consumer side: a consumer processes events, crashes before committing its offset, the group rebalances, and the new owner re-reads from the last commit — everything since is redelivered. In the lab this exact scenario yields ~148 duplicate deliveries with commit-after-batch, or ~258 lost events with commit-before.

Now the honest version of "exactly-once": Kafka transactions make read-process-write within Kafka atomic — offsets and output records commit together (Confluent: exactly-once semantics in Kafka). They do not cover the email your consumer sent, the row it wrote to Postgres, or the charge it made against a card. For external effects, the real mechanism is at-least-once delivery + idempotent processing: carry a unique event ID, and make the consumer's effect a no-op on the second delivery (INSERT … ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING on the event ID; "have I sent email for payment 4711?" checked in the same transaction as the send-record). Exactly-once is not a delivery guarantee you receive; it is an end-to-end property you construct.

5 · Retention and compaction: two shapes of memory

Time/size retention keeps everything for N days (or N GB) then deletes oldest-first — the log as a bounded history buffer. Log compaction instead keeps, forever, the latest record per key, deleting overwritten predecessors (Kafka docs: log compaction). A compacted topic is a changelog: replay it and you reconstruct current state exactly — the lab rebuilds identical state from 1,766 compacted entries instead of 30,000 raw updates, 17× less replay.

This is what makes change data capture (CDC) work: tools like Debezium tail a database's replication log and publish every row change to a topic — your database becomes a stream, and caches, search indexes, and warehouses become mere consumers that can be rebuilt by replay. (Recognize the move: Facebook drove memcache invalidations from the MySQL commit log back in lesson 0007 — CDC generalizes it.) One preview: writing to your DB and publishing an event as two separate actions can half-fail; the transactional outbox fixes this by writing the event into the same DB transaction and letting CDC publish it. Full treatment next lesson.

6 · Event-driven architecture: choreography vs orchestration

With a log in the middle, services stop calling each other and start reacting. Two styles (Fowler: What do you mean by "Event-Driven"?): choreography — order-service emits OrderPlaced; inventory, payment, email each subscribe and act; nobody coordinates — versus orchestration — a coordinator explicitly commands each step and tracks state. Choreography maximizes autonomy and failure isolation (email being down delays emails, nothing else); its price is that nobody can point at the current state of an order — the truth is smeared across five consumers' offsets, and debugging becomes archaeology. Orchestration centralizes the story at the cost of coupling and a smarter, more critical coordinator. (Lesson 0014 turns this into sagas.)

Either way, events become contracts. A producer that renames a field breaks consumers it has never heard of — at runtime, mid-stream. That is why serious event systems run a schema registry with enforced compatibility rules (Avro/Protobuf; backward-compatible changes only: add optional fields, never remove or retype). Treat an event schema like a public API, because that is exactly what it is — one with subscribers you cannot enumerate.

7 · Event sourcing and CQRS

Event sourcing promotes the log to system-of-record: instead of storing current state ("balance = $90"), store the full sequence of domain events ("opened, deposited 100, withdrew 10") and derive state by folding over them (Fowler: Event Sourcing). A payments ledger is the canonical honest example — accountants have used append-only journals for five centuries precisely because an edit-in-place balance destroys the story of how it got there. You gain a perfect audit trail, time-travel debugging ("what did this account look like Tuesday?"), and the ability to compute new views over old history by replay.

CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) is the natural companion: writes append events; reads hit projections — materialized views built by consumers folding the stream into whatever shape each reader needs (a balance table, a search index, a monthly statement) (Fowler: CQRS). Projections are disposable: bug in the fold logic? Fix it and replay from offset zero.

When is it overkill? Fowler's own warning: most CRUD does not need it. You pay eventual consistency between write and read models, schema-versioning of immutable history (old events never go away, so your code must read every version ever written), and unbounded replay times that force snapshotting. Use it for domains where the history is the value — ledgers, orders, inventory movements — not for user-profile settings.

8 · Backpressure and lag: the one metric that matters

Consumer lag — newest offset produced minus newest offset committed, per partition — is the single health metric of an event pipeline. Latency looks fine (each event processes quickly!) while lag quietly grows because arrival rate exceeds processing rate; LinkedIn built Burrow specifically to judge whether lag is trending rather than just nonzero. Alert on lag growth and on lag translated into time ("consumer is 40 minutes behind"), not on any instantaneous number.

Catch-up options, in escalating order: scale consumers (only works up to partition count — §2); make processing cheaper (batch DB writes, drop per-event work); skip forward and backfill later (fine for metrics, unthinkable for a ledger). And plan for the poison pill: one malformed event a consumer cannot process. Retry forever and that partition — and every key in it — blocks behind one message. The standard fix is a dead-letter queue: after N failed attempts, park the event on a DLQ topic and move on, accepting that the affected key's ordering is now broken until a human replays it (Uber: reliable reprocessing & DLQs).

9 · Failure modes that decide interviews

  • Rebalance storms. A slow consumer misses its session heartbeat → gets kicked → rebalance pauses the whole group → the pause makes others fall behind → more kicks. A group that spends its time reassigning partitions processes nothing. Mitigations: sane max.poll.interval, cooperative (incremental) rebalancing, static membership so a rolling deploy isn't N full rebalances.
  • The lag death spiral, with numbers. Capacity 220k events/s against 200k/s incoming = 20k/s spare. A one-hour consumer outage banks 720M events; at 20k/s spare, catch-up takes 720M / 20k = 36,000 s = 10 hours. With 24-hour retention, one more incident during catch-up and the log starts deleting events you never read. Headroom is not a luxury; it is your recovery-time budget.
  • Schema break mid-stream. A producer deploys a field rename; every consumer starts throwing on every event — a fleet-wide poison pill. This is why compatibility checks live in CI via the registry, not in consumers' try/except blocks.
  • Duplicate fan-out. The §4 crash-redelivery scenario reaches a non-idempotent email consumer: customers get two payment confirmations; a non-idempotent ledger consumer double-posts a charge. Same root cause, wildly different blast radius — idempotency effort should follow blast radius.
  • Compaction ate your history. Someone enables compaction on a topic analytics later needs full history from — every intermediate state is unrecoverable, silently, by design. Compaction keeps state, not history; pick per topic, on purpose, and write it down.

10 · One AI note, then back to fundamentals

The inference platform's usage pipeline is this lesson verbatim: every request emits a usage event (tenant, model, tokens in/out, latency) onto a log; billing, eval, and abuse-detection pipelines are independent consumer groups over the same topic; partitioning by tenant_id gives per-tenant ordering with a hot-tenant skew risk you now know by name; and replay is the killer feature — ship a better quality-scoring model, rewind the eval consumer, re-score last month's traffic without touching serving. Billing consumers are the payments-ledger case: at-least-once plus idempotency on the event ID, because double-billing a tenant is the email-twice failure with an invoice attached. That's the note; §1–§9 are what the interview grades.

11 · Interview talking points

  1. Queue or log — say why. "This fact has multiple audiences over time, so a retained log, not a task queue" beats naming a product first.
  2. Name the topic, the key, and the ordering contract in one breath. "Topic orders, keyed by order_id, so each order's lifecycle is ordered; cross-order ordering doesn't exist and we don't need it."
  3. Do the partition arithmetic (§2): producer MB/s and consumer events/s both, then "the consumer side dominates: ~96 partitions."
  4. Volunteer the skew story (§3) before the interviewer asks: who's the celebrity key and what happens to their partition.
  5. Say "at-least-once plus idempotent consumers" unprompted (§4), and define the idempotency key. Then, if pushed on exactly-once: "Kafka transactions cover Kafka-to-Kafka; external effects need dedup at the edge."
  6. Name lag as your health metric (§8) and give the catch-up math (§9) — spare capacity as recovery-time budget is a strong-hire sentence.
  7. Close with the contract: schema registry, backward-compatible evolution only, checked in CI — events are APIs with unknown subscribers.
