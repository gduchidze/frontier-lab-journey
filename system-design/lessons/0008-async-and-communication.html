Lesson 0008 · Phase 1 — Distributed Systems Core

Queues, Backpressure, and Communication

Why every system hides a queue somewhere — and why the queue must be allowed to say no.

Mission tie. The reported Anthropic system-design prompt about batching has a nasty detail: synchronous callers are waiting while the batch fills. That makes the request queue the real protagonist — how deep may it grow, whom do you reject, and what do you tell them? Admission control is the heart of that question, and this lesson is admission control — taught on classic systems (checkouts, payments, feeds) because the physics is identical everywhere. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · Why async: the queue as shock absorber

A synchronous call chains your fate to the callee: if the email service is slow, your checkout is slow. Putting a queue between producer and consumer decouples them — the producer returns immediately, the consumer drains at its own pace, and short bursts get absorbed instead of toppling the service. That is the entire pitch for asynchronism. (Primer §14: asynchronism)

The canonical example is an e-commerce order. The only thing that must happen synchronously when a customer clicks "buy" is recording the order and reserving payment. Everything else — confirmation email, invoice PDF, warehouse pick ticket, loyalty points, analytics, fraud re-scoring — goes onto a queue and happens seconds later. The user sees a 200 ms confirmation instead of waiting on six downstream systems, and a two-minute outage in the invoice generator loses nothing: the messages wait.

Now the dark side: a queue is a place where latency hides. Nothing errors, dashboards stay green, throughput looks fine — while every enqueued item quietly waits longer. Run the numbers on a flash sale: your order pipeline drains 500 orders/s; the sale spikes arrivals to 2,000/s for 60 seconds. The queue absorbs 90,000 excess orders — heroic! — but at 500/s it takes another 180 seconds to drain, so the last "instantly confirmed" customer gets their pick ticket 3+ minutes late. A queue buys you time to survive a burst; it does not create capacity. If arrivals exceed the drain rate on average, no queue is big enough — you have only chosen where the failure appears: in wait time instead of error rate.

2 · The queueing zoo: message queues vs task queues

Two families, one idea at different altitudes (Primer: message & task queues):

SystemKindModelTypical use
KafkaMessage queue (log)Append-only partitioned log; consumers track offsets; messages retained for daysEvent streams, feed fan-out, analytics pipelines, replayable history
RabbitMQMessage queue (broker)Smart broker routes to queues; message deleted on ackWork distribution, RPC-over-queue, per-message routing
SQSMessage queue (managed)Hosted queue with visibility timeouts and redeliveryDecoupling AWS services without operating a broker
Celery / SidekiqTask queueWraps a broker to run jobs: workers, retries, schedules, results"Resize this image", "send this email", cron-like background work

The distinction that matters in interviews: a message queue moves and buffers data between services; a task queue is one level up — it manages the execution of background jobs (worker pools, retry policy, result storage) on top of a broker. Kafka's log model adds a second superpower: because messages are retained rather than deleted, new consumers can replay history — which is why it was born at LinkedIn to feed the same activity events to many independent systems.

Also know the failure mode of the whole category: an async pipeline turns "user sees an error" into "work silently piles up." You must monitor queue depth and consumer lag as first-class health metrics (section 6 explains why depth is the best early-warning signal you have).

3 · Delivery semantics: pick your poison

Whenever a broker (or any network) sits in the middle, delivery is a bet, and you must pick which way to lose:

SemanticGuaranteeFailure modeStandard fix
At-most-onceDelivered 0 or 1 timesMessages silently lostAccept it — only for tolerable loss (metrics, logs, presence pings)
At-least-onceDelivered 1+ timesDuplicates on retry/redeliveryIdempotency keys — duplicates become no-ops
Exactly-onceDelivered exactly 1 timeMostly a lie above the transportAt-least-once + idempotent consumer

Why at-least-once is the default: the ambiguous case is unavoidable. A worker takes "charge the card $99", the charge succeeds, and the worker crashes before acknowledging. The broker cannot distinguish "processed but didn't ack" from "never processed", so it redelivers — and a naive consumer charges twice. The industry-standard fix is the idempotency key: the client attaches a unique key per logical operation, the server records processed keys, and a duplicate returns the stored result instead of re-executing. This is literally how the Stripe API makes payment retries safe. (Stripe: designing robust and predictable APIs with idempotency)

Exactly-once claims deserve suspicion. Kafka transactions genuinely provide it within Kafka's own boundary (consume-process-produce between topics). But the moment your consumer touches the outside world — a payment API, an email, a row in someone else's database — you are back to at-least-once plus idempotency. In an interview, the winning sentence is: "I'll take at-least-once delivery and make the consumer idempotent with an idempotency key."

4 · Backpressure: teach the system to say no

Backpressure is the discipline of pushing overload back toward the source instead of absorbing it into an unbounded queue. Without it, overload has two endings, both bad:

  • The unbounded queue ending. Depth grows until memory runs out (the worker OOMs and now you've lost the queue too) or until wait times exceed all client timeouts — at which point the server is burning 100% of its capacity completing requests whose callers already gave up. This zombie work is the signature of a queue-collapse outage: full utilization, zero goodput. (Google SRE book: addressing cascading failures)
  • The blocking ending. If instead the server blocks callers when busy, the queue doesn't vanish — it relocates into the callers' threads, filling their thread pools, and the overload climbs the call graph one tier at a time. That is a cascading failure in slow motion.

The fix is a three-part contract:

  • Bound every queue. A bound converts "invisible latency growth" into an explicit, handleable event: the queue is full. Choosing the bound is a latency decision, not a memory decision — section 6 gives you the formula.
  • Reject early and cheaply. When full, fail fast with 503 Service Unavailable (service saturated) or 429 Too Many Requests (this client over quota), plus a Retry-After header. A rejection in 1 ms is kinder than a success in 40 s: the caller can retry, use a fallback, or degrade gracefully — a checkout that says "try again in a few seconds" beats one that spins forever.
  • Tell clients how to come back. Rejection without retry guidance just converts overload into an uncoordinated retry mob — which is section 5.

5 · Retries: the cure that causes the disease

Retries are the standard response to transient failure, and they are also how systems commit suicide. Two mechanisms to know cold:

The thundering herd. A service blips for 2 seconds and rejects 10,000 in-flight clients. If every client retries after exactly 1 s, the service — just recovering — is hit by a synchronized 10,000-request wave, falls over again, rejects again, and the wave repeats forever. The fix has two halves: exponential backoff (wait 1 s, 2 s, 4 s… so pressure decays) and — the half people forget — jitter. With full jitter each client sleeps a uniformly random time in [0, cap], which smears the wave flat. AWS's classic analysis showed backoff-with-full-jitter dramatically cuts both total calls and time-to-recovery versus backoff alone. (AWS: exponential backoff and jitter)

Retry amplification. Retries multiply through layers. If the mobile app retries 3×, the API gateway retries 3×, and the backend client library retries 3×, one user tap can become 3 × 3 × 3 = 27 requests to the bottom service — precisely when it is least able to serve them. Google's rules from production: retry at one layer only, cap attempts (~3), and enforce a per-service retry budget — e.g., retries may add at most 10% extra load; beyond that, fail the request through to the user. (Google SRE book: handling overload)

The lab makes this visceral: the same overload that a jittered-backoff client population absorbs gracefully becomes a self-sustaining storm under naive immediate retries.

6 · Little's law and the utilization hockey stick

One formula governs every queue, from checkout pipelines to supermarket lines: L = λW — average items in the system equals arrival rate times average time in the system. It is distribution-free and shockingly useful in three directions:

  • Forecast the wait. Rearranged, W = L/λ. Your payment worker pool drains λ = 500 jobs/s and the queue shows L = 3,000 jobs: every newly enqueued job will wait ≈ 6 s. No percentile dashboard needed — current depth ÷ drain rate is next-minute latency. Depth is the leading indicator (latency percentiles report the past), which is why mature systems alert and autoscale on it.
  • Size the bound. If your SLO tolerates at most 2 s of queueing and you drain 500/s, the queue bound is λW = 500 × 2 = 1,000. Deeper than that, and you are storing requests you have already decided to serve too late — reject them instead.
  • Size the pool. Need to hold W = 200 ms of processing at λ = 500/s with single-threaded workers averaging 50 ms/job? L = λ × 0.05 = 25 jobs in service → ~25 workers minimum, before headroom.

And headroom matters brutally, because waiting explodes near saturation. For a single-server queue with random arrivals (M/M/1), mean queueing delay scales like ρ/(1−ρ) times the service time, where ρ is utilization:

Utilization ρQueue wait (× service time)
50%1×
80%4×
90%9×
95%19×
99%99×

Nearly flat until ~70–80%, then a hockey stick: going from 90% to 95% utilization doubles the wait. This is lesson 0001's saturation cliff, now with a mechanism — the queue in front of the busy resource is where the cliff lives. Practical rules: plan capacity so hot paths sit below ~70–80% utilization, treat "we're at 95% and fine" as a lit fuse, and treat rising depth as the fire alarm.

7 · Load shedding: choosing whom to fail

When overload arrives anyway, rejecting uniformly is leaving money on the table — traffic is not equally valuable. Load shedding is dropping the cheapest-to-lose work first, by explicit priority:

  • Criticality tiers. An e-commerce site under stress sheds recommendation widgets and "customers also bought" before search, and search before checkout and payment. Netflix formalized this as request taxonomies (user-initiated vs prefetch vs telemetry) and sheds low-criticality traffic first, keeping play-button presses alive through regional incidents. (Netflix: prioritized load shedding)
  • Batch behind interactive. Background reprocessing, report generation, and bulk imports should yield instantly to human-facing requests — they have hours of deadline slack; a browsing user has ~1 s of patience.
  • Graceful degradation. Shedding a feature beats shedding a user: serve the cached-but-stale product page, skip the personalization pass, show the feed without ranking. Partial answers keep revenue flowing while full answers are unaffordable.

The design smell to avoid: priorities discovered during the incident. The tier of every endpoint — and what gets shed first — should be written down before the traffic arrives (your project step below does exactly this).

8 · Communication: the protocol choices behind all of it

TCP vs UDP. TCP gives ordered, reliable, congestion-controlled, retransmitted bytes — at the cost of waiting for late packets (one lost packet stalls everything behind it until retransmission). UDP just sends datagrams. The decision rule: choose UDP when late data is worse than lost data — live video and voice calls, multiplayer game state, high-volume telemetry. A video frame from 2 seconds ago is garbage; skip it and render the next one. Choose TCP when every byte matters and order matters: APIs, storage replication, payments. (Primer §15: communication)

REST vs gRPC. Two defaults for two audiences:

REST (HTTP + JSON)gRPC (HTTP/2 + protobuf)
Best forPublic APIs, loose couplingInternal service-to-service traffic
ContractConvention + OpenAPI (optional)Enforced .proto schema, generated clients
PayloadHuman-readable text, largerCompact binary, faster to parse
StreamingBolted on (SSE, WebSockets)First-class: client, server, bidirectional
Debuggingcurl and eyeballsNeeds tooling (grpcurl, reflection)
CouplingLoose — clients evolve freelyTighter — schema shared, versioned carefully

The standard production answer: REST at the public edge (partners and browsers need loose coupling and debuggability), gRPC inside the datacenter (you own both ends, so take the performance, type safety, and streaming).

Idempotency is a design property, not an afterthought. Look back at this lesson: brokers redeliver (§3), clients back off and retry (§5), gateways retry, TCP retransmits. A distributed system is a machine for generating duplicates, so every state-changing operation must be designed to survive being applied twice. HTTP encodes this in its verbs — GET, PUT, DELETE are defined as idempotent; POST is not, which is exactly why payment APIs layer idempotency keys onto POST. Bolting idempotency on later means auditing every write path in the company; designing it in means one key column and one uniqueness check.

9 · One paragraph of AI flavor

Everything above transfers verbatim to serving models: an inference gateway is a bounded admission queue in front of expensive workers — 429/503 with Retry-After on overload, interactive requests prioritized over batch jobs, and autoscaling driven by queue depth because (per §6) depth is the earliest overload signal available. When Phase 4 builds this gateway for real, it will be this lesson wearing a GPU costume; nothing new will need to be invented.

10 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson in a live design round:

  • Introduce a queue with both halves of the trade. "I'll decouple X from Y with a queue to absorb bursts — and I'll bound it, because a queue is where latency hides." Naming the downside unprompted is a strong senior signal.
  • Volunteer delivery semantics. The moment a broker appears, say: "At-least-once delivery, consumers idempotent via idempotency keys. I won't claim exactly-once across external side effects." That single sentence closes off an entire class of follow-up traps.
  • State the admission policy as numbers. "Queue bound = drain rate × queueing budget from the SLO (Little's law); when full, 503 + Retry-After; clients retry with exponential backoff and full jitter, capped at 3 attempts." Concrete beats hand-wavy "we'd add rate limiting."
  • Use depth as your observability answer. When asked "how do you know it's healthy?", lead with queue depth / consumer lag as the leading indicator, then latency percentiles as the lagging one.
  • Pre-declare shed order. "Under overload we shed in this order: telemetry, recommendations, search, never checkout." Shows you've thought about failure before being asked.
  • Justify protocol picks in one clause each. "REST at the edge for loose coupling; gRPC internally for streaming and typed contracts; UDP only where late is worse than lost."
  • Beware the utilization trap. If the interviewer offers "the service runs at 95% utilization, isn't that efficient?", the answer is the hockey stick: at 95%, waits are ~19× service time and one small burst tips you over; healthy hot paths run at 60–80%.

12 · Project step

Give the spine project — the LLM inference gateway — its admission policy: one short document, four decisions. (1) The queue bound, derived via Little's law from your lesson-0001 SLO: if TTFT p99 < 1 s at λ = 1,000 RPS and you budget ~0.5 s of that for queueing, the depth cap is L = λW = 1,000 × 0.5 = 500. (2) The rejection code: 429 when one client exceeds its quota, 503 when the service itself is saturated. (3) Retry guidance in the response: Retry-After header plus "exponential backoff with jitter, max 3 attempts" in the error body. (4) Priority tiers: which traffic classes exist (interactive, batch), the admission order, and which tier is shed first. Bring it to the next session for critique — defending the numbers matters more than the numbers.
