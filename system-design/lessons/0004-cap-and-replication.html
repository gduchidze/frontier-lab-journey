CAP, Consistency, and Replication

What you actually choose during a partition — and what replication lag does to your users the rest of the time.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design rounds probe distributed-systems fundamentals hard: any prompt with more than one datacenter in it — "design a multi-region API," "design a chat-history store" — gets the same follow-up: "a region goes dark; what do your users see?" Strong candidates answer per component: this store is CP, that one is AP with read-your-writes, here is the lag budget, here is the failover story. This lesson gives you that entire answer. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · CAP, stated correctly

The popular reading — "pick two of Consistency, Availability, Partition tolerance" — is wrong, and interviewers use it as a filter. On a real network, partitions are not optional: cables get cut, switches die, a garbage-collecting node looks dead for 20 seconds. Since P is forced on you, the theorem's real content is narrower and sharper: while a partition is happening, each request must choose Consistency or Availability. When the network is healthy, you can have both. (Primer: CAP theorem; Eric Brewer's own retrospective, "CAP Twelve Years Later")

  • CP — refuse rather than lie. The minority side of a partition stops answering (errors or timeouts) so it can never serve stale data. Products: ZooKeeper and etcd (cluster coordination, lock services), HBase, most bank ledgers. If two nodes can disagree about an account balance, "available" is worse than "down" — a double-spent balance is corruption, not a UX bug.
  • AP — answer, reconcile later. Every node keeps serving its possibly-stale copy and conflicts are merged after the partition heals. Products: DNS (the canonical AP system — stale records for hours, by design), Cassandra and Riak, Amazon's Dynamo. The motivating example in the Dynamo paper is the shopping cart: Amazon decided a cart that briefly shows a re-added deleted item costs less than a cart that refuses writes during an outage — rejecting an "add to cart" is rejecting revenue.

Note the granularity: systems aren't CP or AP — operations are. A payment system can be CP on the ledger and AP on the "recently viewed items" widget. Saying that sentence in an interview is worth more than a paragraph of theory.

2 · PACELC: the trade you make every day

CAP only speaks about partitions, which are rare. PACELC (Abadi, 2012) covers the other 99.99% of the time: if Partitioned, trade Availability vs Consistency; Else, trade Latency vs Consistency. To be strongly consistent, a write must wait for confirmation from other replicas — a synchronous round trip (500 µs same-DC, 150 ms cross-ocean, per Lesson 0001) added to every single write. Refuse to wait and you are inconsistent for the length of the replication lag.

SystemDuring partitionElse (normal operation)
Dynamo / Cassandra / RiakPA — stay availableEL — answer fast, tolerate staleness
ZooKeeper / etcd / HBasePC — refuse minority sideEC — pay latency for consistency
Google SpannerPC — quorum requiredEC — commit waits out clock uncertainty

Spanner is the instructive extreme: Google pays for GPS and atomic clocks in every datacenter precisely so the "wait for consistency" penalty is small enough to afford globally. Consistency is never free — the rich just buy it in bulk.

3 · The consistency spectrum

"Consistency" is a dial, not a switch. From cheapest to most expensive (Primer: consistency patterns):

LevelPromiseTypical home
WeakReads may or may not see the write; best effort, no promise of convergenceLive video, VoIP, multiplayer game position — a lost update is better than a late one
EventualStop writing and all replicas converge, on no particular deadlineDNS, search indexes, S3 metadata propagation, email delivery, like-counters
Strong (linearizable)Every read sees the latest write, as if one copy existedPayments, inventory decrement, unique-username claims, coordination/locks

Worked failure mode, e-commerce flavor: a warehouse has 1 unit left. Two buyers hit "purchase" through two replicas that haven't converged. Eventual consistency happily sells the unit twice; you discover it at pick-and-pack and one customer gets an apology email. That's why inventory decrement is strong even at companies whose catalogs, reviews, and recommendations are all eventual. The dial is set per operation, by the cost of its worst anomaly.

4 · Session guarantees: the practical middle ground

Full strong consistency is usually overkill; raw eventual consistency produces anomalies users actually notice. Between them sit session guarantees — promises scoped to one user's session (DDIA ch. 5, "Problems with Replication Lag"):

  • Read-your-writes. You always see your own writes; other people's reads may lag. The classic bug: a user posts a comment, the page reloads in 200 ms, the read hits a follower that's 500 ms behind — their comment has "vanished." They repost. Now it's there twice. Implementations: route a user's reads to the leader for N seconds after their write; or remember the user's last-write timestamp and only read from replicas that have caught up to it; or sticky-route each user to one region.
  • Monotonic reads. Time never runs backwards across your reads. Anomaly: refresh a social feed and a comment appears; refresh again — routed to a more stale replica — and it's gone. Fix: pin each session to one replica (e.g., hash of user ID → replica), so a user may lag but never time-travels.

These cost almost nothing in latency compared to global strong consistency, and they eliminate the anomalies humans actually complain about. "Read-your-writes via session pinning, and here's what it costs the leader" is a precise, senior-sounding answer; the lab makes you measure that cost.

5 · Leader–follower replication and the perils of failover

The default topology for almost every RDBMS deployment (MySQL, Postgres): all writes go to one leader; followers replicate the change stream and serve reads. The key decision is how the leader acknowledges (DDIA ch. 5):

  • Asynchronous (common): ack the client immediately, replicate in the background. Fast writes — but every follower read races the replication lag, and a leader crash loses the writes still in flight.
  • Synchronous: ack only after a follower confirms. No loss — but one slow follower stalls all writes; fully synchronous to many followers is impractical.
  • Semi-synchronous: at least one follower confirms, the rest replicate async. The production compromise (MySQL semi-sync).

Failover is where this topology bites. The leader dies; a follower is promoted. Three classic wounds:

  • Lost writes. If the promoted follower was behind, writes the old leader acknowledged are simply gone. GitHub's October 2018 incident is the canonical case study: a 43-second network blip triggered a cross-country MySQL failover, seconds of writes existed only on the demoted side, and full reconciliation took over 24 hours of degraded service.
  • Premature promotion. The "dead" leader was just slow (GC pause, network blip). It comes back still believing it's the leader — see split-brain, section 8.
  • Wrong timeout. Fail over too eagerly and you churn leaders during load spikes (which is when lag — and thus data loss — is largest); too lazily and you extend the outage. There is no correct constant, only a tuned one.

6 · Multi-leader replication and conflict pain

Leader–leader (multi-leader): several nodes accept writes and replicate to each other. Nearly mandatory for multi-region products — a European buyer should not push every cart update across an ocean (150 ms each way) — and for offline-capable clients (calendar apps: your phone and laptop are both "leaders" while offline). The price: two leaders can accept conflicting writes to the same key, and something must resolve them:

  • Last-write-wins (LWW): compare timestamps, discard the loser. Simple, and it silently destroys data — with clock skew between datacenters, "last" doesn't even mean last. Fine for a view-counter cache; unforgivable for orders.
  • Application-level merge: keep both versions, let code or the user reconcile (version-vector "siblings" in Riak/early Dynamo — the twice-re-added cart item comes from a cart merge).
  • CRDTs: data structures (counters, sets, collaborative-text sequences) that mathematically guarantee all replicas converge regardless of delivery order — the machinery under collaborative editors like Figma and Google Docs-style apps.

Rule of thumb for interviews: don't volunteer multi-leader unless the prompt demands multi-region active-active writes or offline clients — then immediately name your conflict story. Proposing multi-leader without one is a red flag.

7 · Quorums: R + W > N

The leaderless (Dynamo-style) alternative drops the leader entirely: write to all N replicas, acknowledge once W confirm; read from R replicas and keep the freshest version. The guarantee is pure pigeonhole arithmetic: if R + W > N, any read set must overlap any acknowledged write set in at least one replica, so the read cannot miss the latest write. Not probability — arithmetic.

N = 3Overlap guaranteed?Character
W = 1, R = 1No (sum 2)Fastest, stale reads common — cache-like
W = 2, R = 2Yes (sum 4)The classic balanced default
W = 3, R = 1Yes (sum 4)Slow, fragile writes; cheap fast reads
W = 1, R = 3Yes (sum 4)Cheap fast writes; expensive reads

Tuning is workload-driven: a write-heavy ingest (logistics tracking events) leans W low / R high; a read-heavy catalog leans the opposite. Two honest caveats worth saying aloud: quorum overlap alone is not full linearizability (concurrent writes still need version resolution), and "sloppy quorums" — where, during a partition, writes land on stand-in nodes for availability — deliberately break the overlap guarantee until hinted handoff repairs it. Cassandra exposes all of this as per-query tunable consistency: ONE, QUORUM, ALL.

8 · Failover patterns and split-brain

Two standard shapes for redundancy (Primer: availability patterns):

  • Active-passive: a standby takes over when heartbeats stop. Simple to reason about; costs idle hardware plus a promotion gap (seconds to minutes of writes refused). Interviewers may ask for your RTO (how long until recovered) and RPO (how much data you may lose — for async replication, the lag at crash time).
  • Active-active: all nodes serve traffic behind a load balancer. Better utilization and instant "failover" (the LB just stops routing to the dead node) — but if both sides accept writes, you have bought multi-leader's conflict problems (section 6).

The nightmare shared by both: split-brain. The "dead" node wasn't dead — just partitioned or pausing — and now two leaders accept conflicting writes for the same data. Both believe they're right; both are corrupting state. Defenses: quorum-based election (a node may only lead while a majority acknowledges it — the minority side of any partition therefore cannot elect a leader; this is why coordination clusters have 3 or 5 nodes, never 2 or 4) and fencing (make the demoted leader physically unable to write: revoke its storage token, or STONITH — "shoot the other node in the head" — cut its power). A monotonically increasing fencing token attached to every write lets storage reject the stale leader outright.

9 · Availability math: what nines cost and buy

Availability targets translated into clock time (525,600 minutes/year):

AvailabilityDowntime / yearDowntime / month
99.9% ("three nines")8.8 hours43.8 min
99.99% ("four nines")52.6 minutes4.4 min
99.999% ("five nines")5.3 minutes26 s

Composition is where designs quietly die. In series — the request needs every component — availabilities multiply. A checkout path of load balancer (99.99%) → web tier (99.95%) → payment service (99.9%) → database (99.9%):

0.9999 × 0.9995 × 0.999 × 0.999 ≈ 0.9974  →  99.74%  ≈  22.8 hours down/year

Four individually respectable components compose into something that would embarrass an SLA page. Every dependency you add to the request path subtracts nines — say this sentence whenever anyone (including you) proposes inserting a new synchronous service.

In parallel — any one replica suffices — failure probabilities multiply instead: availability = 1 − Π(1 − aᵢ). Two independent 99.9% payment providers with failover between them:

1 − (0.001)² = 0.999999  →  99.9999%  ≈  32 seconds down/year

Redundancy is how you buy nines back; the caveat that earns senior points is independence — two replicas in the same rack share a power supply, two "providers" sharing an upstream bank share its outages, and correlated failures make the formula optimistic.

10 · One subsection for AI infrastructure

Everything above transfers directly to this course's spine project (the LLM inference gateway) — the dial is still set per store, by worst anomaly: a KV-cache-aware routing table can be eventual/weak, because a stale routing hint costs a cache miss (latency), never a wrong answer; a chat-history store needs read-your-writes, because a user must see the message they just sent while strangers' replicas may lag; a model-weights registry must be strong/CP, because two replicas disagreeing about "current production weights" means silently serving different models to different users — the inventory-double-sell of ML platforms. Same spectrum, same reasoning, new nouns.

13 · Interview talking points

How this lesson deploys in a live design round:

  • Set consistency per operation, out loud. "Orders and inventory are CP; catalog, reviews, and feed are AP with eventual consistency" — one sentence, drawn as two colors on the whiteboard, signals more than any definition of CAP.
  • Never say "eventual consistency" bare. Immediately name the anomaly you're accepting and its mitigation: "…so we need read-your-writes for the author, via leader pinning for 10 s after a write."
  • Preempt the partition question. Before the interviewer asks, state it: "if the region partitions, checkout degrades to read-only (CP), browsing continues stale (AP)." You choosing the degraded mode is the entire point of the question.
  • Do the nines math when the path grows. Someone adds a synchronous service to the request path? "Each 99.9% dependency in series costs us ~9 hours/year" — then propose a cache, a queue, or a fallback to take it off the critical path.
  • Quote R + W > N precisely. And add one caveat (sloppy quorums, or "overlap isn't full linearizability") to show it's knowledge, not a flashcard.
  • For any failover story, volunteer RPO/RTO. "Async replication, so RPO = replication lag at crash, roughly seconds; RTO = detection timeout + promotion, under a minute" — and mention fencing so split-brain is handled before it's asked.
