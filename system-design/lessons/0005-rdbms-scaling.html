RDBMS Scaling: Replication, Sharding, Indexes

One box of Postgres carries you astonishingly far — here is the exact order in which to spend its lives, and why sharding is the last rung of the ladder, not the first.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design rounds still lean on the classics — a feed, a payments ledger, an orders table — because they expose whether you can scale the world's default database. The interviewer is listening for two specific sentences: one that names the index behind every query you promise, and one that defends your shard key against the hot-key problem. Both sentences are the deliverable of this lesson.

1 · ACID, and when you actually need it

ACID is the transaction contract: Atomicity (a multi-statement change lands entirely or not at all), Consistency (declared constraints and invariants hold before and after), Isolation (concurrent transactions cannot observe each other's half-done work), Durability (once committed, a crash cannot un-commit it). (DDIA ch. 7)

You need transactions when several rows must change together and a partial state is unacceptable. The canonical case is checkout: decrement inventory, create the order row, record the charge — if the process dies between steps, you've either sold a phantom item or charged for nothing. Same for moving money between accounts and claiming a unique username. You do not need multi-statement transactions for single-row appends (a log line, a page-view event) — the row insert is already atomic on its own.

Two refinements interviewers reward. First, isolation has levels: most databases default to something weaker than serializable (read committed, snapshot isolation) because full serializability costs throughput — say which level your invariant actually requires. Second, the failure mode of transaction enthusiasm: long transactions hold locks, stall replication, and turn a hot row (a single inventory counter on a flash-sale item) into a global convoy. Transactions are a scalpel, not a blanket.

2 · Indexes: the B-tree you must be able to draw

An index is a B-tree: keys kept sorted in a wide, shallow tree. Do the arithmetic once and keep it forever: an 8 KB page holds roughly 400 branch entries, so depth 3 reaches 400³ ≈ 64 million rows and depth 4 reaches ≈ 25 billion. A point read on a billion-row table is therefore ~4 page touches in O(log n) — and since the top levels stay cached in RAM, it is usually one random SSD read, ~150 µs on the lesson-0001 latency table. A range query descends once to the start of the range, then reads leaves sequentially — this is why "give me this user's messages from last week" can be cheap while "scan everything" never is.

The price is write amplification: every index is one more B-tree that must be updated on every insert and update. A table with five indexes does six structure writes per logical row written; bulk loads slow down, pages split, storage bloats. Indexes are not free reads — they are prepaid reads, billed to the write path.

  • Composite ordering rule. An index on (user_id, created_at) is a phone book sorted by last name, then first. It serves "equality on user_id, then range/sort on created_at" perfectly — and is nearly useless for "range on created_at alone," exactly as a phone book is useless for finding everyone named Anna. Rule: equality columns first, the range/sort column last. This is the leftmost-prefix rule, and the lab makes you feel it as a timing gap.
  • Covering index. If the index contains every column the query touches, the engine never visits the table at all — an index-only scan. Adding one displayed column to an index can delete millions of table lookups per day.
  • Classic self-inflicted wounds. Indexing a two-value column (is_active) buys nothing — half the table still matches. Wrapping the column in a function (WHERE lower(email) = …) silently defeats the index unless the index is on the expression. And a write-heavy table with nine "just in case" indexes is a write-throughput incident scheduled in advance.
Design discipline Every query in the design should name its index. "We fetch the customer's last 50 orders — via the (customer_id, created_at) index" is an answer. "We fetch the orders" is a full table scan waiting to be discovered in the follow-up question.

3 · The scaling ladder — in order, no skipping

When one primary saturates, climb in this order. The ordering is not aesthetic: each rung is cheaper, better understood, and more reversible than the next, and each buys time before the next is needed. (Primer §12: RDBMS)

RungWhat it buysWhat it costs
1 · Read replicasRead throughput, cheaplyReplication lag (lesson 0004); writes still one box
2 · CachingAbsorbs the hot read setInvalidation bugs, stale reads, stampedes
3 · FederationSplit by function: users DB, catalog DB, orders DBNo cross-database joins; per-function ceilings remain
4 · ShardingWrite scaling, at lastEverything in section 6

Anchor number for the whole ladder: a single well-tuned Postgres/MySQL box comfortably serves tens of thousands of reads/sec, low thousands of writes/sec, and terabytes of data. Most systems that "need sharding" on a whiteboard never outgrow rungs 1–2 in production. Saying why you are not sharding yet is a senior-signal sentence.

4 · Rungs 1–2: read replicas and caching

Read replicas. One primary takes all writes and streams its changes (asynchronously, usually) to N copies that serve reads. This fits the shape of almost every consumer workload: an e-commerce site doing 10,000 RPS at a 98:2 read/write ratio has 9,800 reads/sec to distribute and only 200 writes/sec — three replicas absorb the reads, the primary barely notices the writes. The costs: replication lag resurrects lesson 0004's problems — a shopper posts a review, the page reloads from a replica that hasn't heard about it, and the review "disappears" (the read-your-own-writes problem: pin the author's reads to the primary, briefly). And replicas do nothing for write volume: the ceiling is still one box.

Caching. Before touching the database tier again, put the hot read set in memory (cache-aside with Redis/Memcached is the default pattern: read cache → miss → read DB → fill cache). A product page rendered a million times an hour has no business being a query a million times an hour. Costs are the two famous hard problems: invalidation (stale price on a live product page) and the stampede (a hot key expires and ten thousand concurrent misses hit the database at once — bound it with request coalescing or jittered TTLs). Cache layers are covered properly in a later lesson; here they are rung 2 because they are cheap and reversible.

5 · Rung 3: federation — split by function

Federation gives each business function its own database: users in one, product catalog in another, orders in a third. Each gets its own hardware, its own replicas, its own cache, and its own failure domain — the catalog going down no longer takes checkout with it. This is the database-side twin of service decomposition, and it delays sharding by carving the write load into independent slices. (Primer: federation)

The bill: cross-database joins stop existing. "Orders joined to user emails" becomes two queries stitched in application code. Transactions spanning functions (place order + decrement catalog stock) stop being one COMMIT and become a distributed-coordination problem — the usual escape hatches (sagas, outbox pattern) arrive in a later lesson. And federation has a horizon: it splits load between tables, so the single hottest table — always orders, in the end — still lives on one primary. When that one function outgrows one box, only rung 4 is left.

6 · Rung 4: sharding — the last resort, itemized

Sharding splits one table's rows across machines by a shard key. It is the only rung that scales writes for a single table, and it is priced accordingly. First decision: how the key maps to shards. (DDIA ch. 6)

StrategyWinsBreaks
Hash sharding — hash(key) % NEven key spread, no planningRange scans die: adjacent keys live on different machines
Range sharding — key intervals per shardRanges stay local; easy splitsHot ranges: shard by created_at and today's shard takes 100% of writes

Four costs arrive the day you shard, whatever you choose:

  • Hot keys — the celebrity problem. Hashing balances keys, not load. Shard a social network by user_id and the day a celebrity with 100M followers joins, one shard glows while the rest idle; real workloads are Zipf-distributed, where the top 1% of keys can carry a third of the traffic. Mitigations you should be able to say out loud: sub-partition the hot key (append a salt), isolate whales onto dedicated shards, or handle them through a different path entirely. The lab manufactures this with a Zipf workload so you can watch the hot shard emerge.
  • Resharding pain. With naive hash(key) % N, changing N remaps almost everything: going from 4 to 5 shards, a key keeps its shard only when hash % 4 == hash % 5 — true for 4 residues out of 20, i.e. 80% of all rows move. Mitigations: consistent hashing (a later lesson), or the pragmatic industry standard — pre-split into far more logical shards than physical hosts and move whole logical shards, as Notion did with 480 logical shards on 32 hosts.
  • Cross-shard queries become scatter-gather. Any question not anchored on the shard key — "top sellers today" on a customer-sharded orders table — must fan out to every shard and merge. Lesson 0001 told you what fan-out does to tail latency: the query is now as slow as the slowest shard. Cross-shard joins are worse: they effectively move the join into your application. Every sharded design should name the queries it deliberately gave up.
  • Auto-increment IDs break. Every shard happily generates its own 1, 2, 3… — colliding sequences. Real systems switch to structured IDs: Twitter's Snowflake packs a 41-bit timestamp + worker id + sequence into 64 bits; Instagram embedded the shard id in the ID itself, so the ID tells you where the row lives. UUIDs work too, at the cost of index locality.

Real-world anchors worth citing in an interview: Vitess (built to shard YouTube's MySQL, now the engine behind PlanetScale), Notion's workspace-sharded Postgres, Instagram's sharded Postgres. All three chose the shard key by asking one question first: what does the dominant query filter on?

7 · Denormalization: paying writes to buy reads

Normalization optimizes writes (one fact, one place); denormalization copies data so a read that was a three-way join becomes one lookup. You pay every write twice or thrice to make every read cheap — the right trade whenever reads dominate, which is nearly always (100:1 is unremarkable). (Primer: denormalization)

The classic worked example is the social feed. Normalized, "show my feed" is a join across ~200 followees' recent posts, per user, per refresh — unpayable at scale. Denormalized (fan-out on write), each new post is copied into every follower's precomputed feed list: reads become one lookup, but a celebrity posting to 100M followers turns one write into 100M. Twitter's famous answer is the hybrid: fan-out on write for normal accounts, pull-on-read for celebrities, merge at request time. Notice this is the hot-key problem again, wearing a different hat — the same whales break both your shards and your fan-out.

A materialized view is denormalization with the bookkeeping handled by the database: a precomputed query result (say, revenue per seller per day) refreshed on a schedule or incrementally. The failure mode of hand-rolled denormalization is drift — a copy nobody remembered to update on the write path. Every denormalized copy needs a named owner and an update rule, or it will quietly become a lie.

8 · One AI note — the chat messages table

Everything above transfers directly to an AI chat product's messages table; the only new content is the key choice. Shard by conversation_id and rendering one chat is a single shard, but "list all my conversations" scatter-gathers on every sidebar load. Shard by user_id and the whole history page is one shard, but a hugely active account — an org workspace, a bot hammering the API — is your celebrity, and any future shared-conversation feature loses colocation. Where conversations belong to one user, user_id usually wins (it is Notion's workspace_id decision in miniature) — then you defend the whale case out loud, exactly as in section 6.

9 · Interview talking points

How this lesson deploys in a live design round:

  • Open with the ratio. Before choosing any storage move, state the read/write ratio and the working-set size. Every rung of the ladder is chosen by those two numbers.
  • Name the index, every time. For each query in your design, say the index that serves it, in composite-order: "last 50 orders per customer → (customer_id, created_at)." Free seniority signal, costs five seconds.
  • Climb in order, and say why you're stopping. "At 200 writes/sec one primary is bored; replicas plus a cache carry us to ~50× current traffic, so I'm not sharding yet" beats jumping to shards unprompted.
  • If you shard, recite the liturgy unprompted: the key and the dominant query it serves; the hot-key defense; the resharding plan (logical shards ≫ physical hosts); the ID scheme (Snowflake-style); and the cross-shard queries you are knowingly giving up.
  • Trade writes for reads explicitly. When you denormalize, say the sentence: "I'm paying N extra writes to make the hot read O(1), and here is what keeps the copies honest."
  • Keep the anchor numbers loaded: one box ≈ low-thousands of writes/sec and terabytes; B-tree depth 4 ≈ billions of rows ≈ one 150 µs SSD read; % N resharding moves ~80% of rows at 4→5.
