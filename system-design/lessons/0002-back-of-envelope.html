Back-of-Envelope Estimation

Five minutes of powers-of-ten that decide every design choice you make afterward — the full playbook, worked examples, traps, and interview delivery.

Mission tie. The Anthropic prompt from Lesson 0001 — "design a token-generation service that handles 100,000 requests per second" — is unanswerable until you turn its numbers into machines: how many servers is 100k RPS? Does the hot state fit in RAM? Is storage or bandwidth the bottleneck? Estimation is the translator between a prompt's numbers and the boxes you are about to draw. Get it wrong by 100× and every later choice — sharding, caching, replication — is wrong with it. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · Rounding discipline

Napkin math has one rule: powers of ten beat precision. A day has 86,400 seconds — in an interview it has 105. The 14% error you just accepted is noise compared to the 2–10× uncertainty in your traffic assumptions. The napkin-math credo: landing within an order of magnitude is success. This is how the numbers are used inside Google too — Jeff Dean's famous "numbers everyone should know" slide exists precisely so engineers can do this arithmetic in their heads mid-meeting. (Dean, Stanford CS295 talk)

Second rule: state assumptions out loud. "I'll assume 200 bytes per message — text plus metadata. Sound reasonable?" The interviewer is grading the method and the checkpoint, never the third significant digit — and saying it aloud lets them correct you before you build five minutes of math on a bad premise.

Third rule: carry units through every line. "2×10⁸ messages/day × 200 B/message = 4×10¹⁰ B/day." If the units don't cancel to what you wanted, the arithmetic is wrong no matter how confident you feel. Most public estimation blunders are unit errors, not multiplication errors.

3 · The playbook — one sequence for any prompt

Every estimation follows the same spine. Memorize it as a pipeline:

StepFormulaAnchor
1. DAU → avg QPSDAU × actions/user ÷ 1051M req/day ≈ 12 RPS
2. Avg → peak QPSavg × 2–10ask, or assume 5×
3. Read/write splitreads = writes × ratio10:1 typical, 100:1 for feeds
4. Storage/daywrites/day × bytes/item×365/year, ×3 replicas, ×growth
5. Bandwidthbytes/day ÷ 105ingest ≠ egress; egress = ingest × read ratio

In the interview, run it as a fill-in template — literally write this skeleton on the whiteboard and populate it top to bottom:

DAU: ____        actions/user/day: ____   →  requests/day: ____
avg QPS = requests/day ÷ 10⁵ = ____       →  peak QPS (×__): ____
read:write = __:1                          →  read QPS: ____ / write QPS: ____
item size: ____ B                          →  storage/day: ____  →  /year (×365): ____
                                              provisioned (×3 replicas): ____
bandwidth: ingest ____ /s · egress (×read ratio) ____ /s
VERDICT: bottleneck is ________ → design must be ________-first

This whole pipeline is the second thing you do in an interview — right after clarifying requirements — and it should take ≤ 5 minutes. Its outputs (peak QPS, storage class, bandwidth class) are the inputs to every architecture decision that follows. It is the same order taught in Alex Xu's back-of-envelope chapter (ByteByteGo, SDI vol. 1 ch. 2) and drilled in napkin-math.

4 · Worked example — a 10M-DAU social feed

Assume: 10M DAU · 2 posts/user/day · 10 feed loads/user/day, 20 posts each
        1 KB per post (text + metadata) · peak 5×

writes:  10M × 2 = 2×10⁷ posts/day ÷ 10⁵ s = 200 writes/s avg → 1,000 peak
reads:   10M × 10 = 10⁸ feed loads/day = 1,000 loads/s avg → 5,000 peak
         × 20 posts each = 100,000 post-fetches/s at peak → read:write ≈ 100:1
storage: 2×10⁷ × 1 KB = 20 GB/day → ×365 ≈ 7 TB/year (×3 replicas ≈ 21 TB)

Read the numbers like an architect. 200 writes/s is nothing — a laptop Postgres absorbs it. 100,000 post-fetches/s at peak is everything — that 100:1 read:write ratio is the single number that mandates a cache tier and precomputed timelines (fan-out-on-write), which is exactly how Twitter's original timeline service was shaped (Krikorian, Timelines at Scale). The estimate didn't just size the system — it chose the architecture.

5 · Worked example — e-commerce checkout and payments

Assume: 5M DAU marketplace · 30 page views/user/day · 1 in 20 users buys/day
        order record 2 KB · each order → ~5 payment-service calls
        Black Friday peak: 10× (retail peaks are violent — use the top of the range)

browse:  5M × 30 = 1.5×10⁸ views/day = 1,500 QPS avg → 15,000 QPS Black Friday
orders:  5M ÷ 20 = 250k orders/day = 2.5 orders/s avg → 25/s peak
payment: 25 × 5 = 125 payment calls/s at peak
storage: 250k × 2 KB = 500 MB/day → ~180 GB/year of orders

Two lessons hide in this napkin. First, the read side and the write side live in different worlds: 15,000 QPS of catalog browsing needs CDN + cache + replicas, while 25 orders/s needs almost no scale at all — one well-run relational database holds years of orders. What the order path needs instead is durability, idempotency, and audit — qualities, not quantities. This is why real payment systems obsess over idempotency keys rather than sharding (Stripe: idempotency in payment APIs).

Second, peak factor is a business fact, not a math constant. A social feed peaks ~2–5× at evening hours; retail peaks 10×+ on promo days; payroll peaks on the 1st of the month; logistics peaks with shopping seasons. Asking "is your peak a daily rhythm or an event spike?" is a clarifying question that earns points on its own.

6 · Worked example — Instagram-scale photo storage and CDN

Rerun the feed with one changed assumption — users attach photos — and watch every conclusion flip:

Assume: 10M DAU · 2 photos/user/day · 500 KB/photo · reads 100:1

ingest:     10M × 2 × 500 KB = 10¹³ B = 10 TB/day   (vs 20 GB/day of text — 500×)
replicated: × 3 for durability → 30 TB/day provisioned
per year:   10 TB × 365 ≈ 3.6 PB/year raw → ~11 PB provisioned
bandwidth:  ingest 10 TB/day ÷ 10⁵ s ≈ 100 MB/s sustained
            egress at 100:1 reads ≈ 10 GB/s → CDN is not optional

Three multipliers turn "manageable" into "petabytes," and interviewers check whether you apply them unprompted: replication ×3 (or ~1.5× with erasure coding — say which you assumed), metadata and indexes ~×2 on structured data (rows carry indexes, write-ahead logs, fragmentation), and growth (a 5-year horizon at 2×/year is ~30× — a design sized only for today expires in 18 months).

The architectural consequence is the classic metadata/blob split: small hot rows (post text, photo IDs, user graph) in a database; large immutable blobs in object storage behind a CDN. That is Instagram's actual shape — a modest relational core beside an ocean of objects (Instagram Engineering: What Powers Instagram) — and the same split appears in every photo, video, and document system you will ever design.

7 · Common estimation traps — six ways napkins lie

TrapSymptomGuard rail
Designing for average, not peaksystem dies at 8 pm or on Black Fridayalways multiply by 2–10× and say the factor
Bits vs bytes8× error on every bandwidth figurenetworks quote bits (Gbps); storage quotes bytes (GB)
Forgetting replication + overheadstorage bill 3–6× the estimateraw × 3 (replicas) × ~2 (indexes, logs)
Per-day vs per-second confusionanswers off by exactly 10⁵write units on every line; make them cancel
Estimating means when tails rulemeets average SLO, fails p99 (Lesson 0001)capacity from peak, latency from percentiles
Silent assumptionsfive minutes of math on a wrong premisevoice every assumption as a question

A seventh, subtler one: false precision as a tell. Saying "13,889 QPS" instead of "~15k" signals you computed 1.2B ÷ 86,400 by long division and missed the point. Precision beyond one significant figure is not rigor — it is noise wearing rigor's clothes.

8 · From estimates to architecture decisions

The whole reason to estimate is that each output crosses (or doesn't cross) a threshold that opens or closes a design door:

Estimate saysThreshold crossedDecision it drives
< ~10k QPSone commodity box's worthload-balanced pair; no sharding conversation yet
≫ 10k QPS peakmultiple machines mandatoryhorizontal scaling, stateless services, LB tiers
Hot set < a few hundred GBfits in RAMcache the whole thing; DB becomes system of record
Read:write ≥ 10:1read-dominatedcache tier, replicas, CDN, precomputed views
Write-heavy streamingest-dominatedappend-friendly stores (LSM), queues, batch compaction
Data in TBs, single tableone node's disk/IOPS ceilingpartitioning/sharding — pick a key now
Blobs in PBsbeyond databases entirelyobject storage + metadata DB + CDN split
Egress ≫ ingestbandwidth cost dominatesCDN, compression, regional replication

This table is the bridge to the rest of Phase 1: Lesson 0003 (traffic and the edge) is the top rows; the storage lessons are the bottom rows. When you estimate in an interview, narrate the doors as they open: "500 MB/day of orders — storage is a non-problem, so I'll spend my complexity budget on the read path."

9 · The same napkin, aimed at AI serving (one taste)

Every technique above transfers unchanged to LLM infrastructure — only the constants change. Two formulas turn a model card into a capacity plan: weights (bf16) = params × 2 bytes, and KV cache per token = 2 × layers × kv_heads × head_dim × 2 bytes. Applied: a 70B model is 140 GB of weights — it does not even fit one 80 GB H100, forcing tensor parallelism across two GPUs, which then leave only ~20 GB of memory for roughly 7 concurrent 8k-token sessions (~2.7 GB of KV cache each). An 8B model (16 GB weights, ~1.1 GB per 8k session) fits ~60 sessions on one GPU. Same five lines of arithmetic as Section 4; ~15× difference in hardware. The lab drills these alongside the classic problems, and Phase 4 builds the full serving design on top of them.

11 · Project step

Take the SLO sheet you wrote for the LLM inference gateway in Lesson 0001 and add a capacity line using Section 3's template: from your sustained RPS target and an assumed in-flight duration, estimate concurrent sessions; from an assumed model size and context length, compute memory demand and a machine count (Section 9's formulas). Then add one classic-systems line: estimated request-log volume per day (requests × ~1 KB structured log), with replication, and where it lives after 90 days. Bring both numbers — your teacher will attack the assumptions, which is the point.

12 · Interview talking points

How to run the five-minute estimation slot, in order:

  • Announce the transition. After clarifying: "Let me spend three minutes on numbers so the design has a target." Interviewers score candidates who structure their own time.
  • Assumptions as questions. "Assume 10M DAU and 5× peak — fair?" Every assumption voiced is a correction opportunity and a collaboration signal.
  • Round shamelessly, and say the tolerance. "Within 10× is all we need here." Never apologize for imprecision; explain it.
  • Tie every number to a door. Numbers without decisions are trivia. "100:1 reads — so a cache tier and precomputed feeds, and the write path stays boring."
  • Close with a verdict line. "So: ~15k QPS peak, ~10 TB/day of media, egress-dominated — the design has to be CDN-first." That one sentence is the contract for the next 30 minutes.
  • If pushed on precision, do one exact calculation to show you can, then return to powers of ten. The skill being tested is judgment about when precision pays.
  • Run the red-flag list silently before moving on: peak? bits/bytes? replication? per-day/per-second? tail? Five seconds of checking beats five minutes of confidently wrong arithmetic.
