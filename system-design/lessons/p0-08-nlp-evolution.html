NLP Evolution: From Characters to Attention

Four ideas — subwords, embeddings, seq2seq, attention — that turned text into something a GPU can serve.

Mission tie. Every capacity number in the Anthropic loop — tokens/sec throughput, context-window limits, KV-cache sizing, the bill itself — is denominated in tokens, and the tokenizer defines what a token is. Karpathy's rule of thumb: a surprising fraction of everything weird about LLMs traces back to tokenization. This lesson builds the unit your entire spine project is priced in. (Karpathy — Let's build the GPT Tokenizer)

1 · The model never sees text

A neural net consumes integers, not strings. So the first design decision in any language model is the tokenizer: the mapping from text to a sequence of IDs from a fixed vocabulary. Three obvious choices, three different failure modes:

GranularityVocab sizeSequence lengthUnseen words
Characters/bytes~100–256Very long (1 char = 1 token)Always covered
Words100k–1M+ShortOOV → <UNK>, information lost
Subwords (BPE)32k–128kMedium (~3–4 chars/token)Falls back to smaller pieces

Word-level dies on morphology, typos, names, and other languages (the out-of-vocabulary problem). Character-level covers everything but makes sequences 3–4× longer — and sequence length is what attention, memory, and latency are all priced in. Subwords are the engineered compromise, introduced for machine translation by Sennrich et al. 2015.

2 · BPE in four lines

Byte-pair encoding is a compression algorithm from 1994 repurposed as a tokenizer trainer: start from characters, then repeatedly merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a new vocabulary token, until you hit a vocab budget. Encoding new text = replay the merges in training order. Decoding = concatenate. Watch it run on the classic example:

text: a a a b d a a a b a c            (11 tokens)
pairs: (a,a)=4  (a,b)=2  (b,d)=1  (d,a)=1  (b,a)=1  (a,c)=1

merge 1: (a,a) -> "aa"    ->  aa a b d aa a b a c    (9 tokens)
merge 2: (aa,a) -> "aaa"  ->  aaa b d aaa b a c      (7 tokens)
merge 3: (aaa,b) -> "aaab" -> aaab d aaab a c        (5 tokens)

3 merges: vocab grows by 3, sequence shrinks 11 -> 5 (2.2x)

That is the entire trick. Frequent words end up as single tokens; rare words shatter into familiar fragments; nothing is ever out of vocabulary because the worst case is falling back to characters. Karpathy's minbpe is the cleanest reference implementation — ~100 lines.

4 · Real tokenizers ship differently

Production tokenizers refine the toy in three ways. First, they operate on bytes, not characters, so all of Unicode is covered by a 256-symbol base alphabet (GPT-2's move). Second, a regex pre-splits text so merges never cross word boundaries or mix letters with punctuation. Third, they are trained on terabytes, not paragraphs. (tiktoken, SentencePiece — the latter treats text as a raw stream, no pre-tokenization, which is why Llama handles whitespace differently.)

TokenizerVocabNotes
GPT-2 (byte-level BPE)50,257256 bytes + 50k merges + <|endoftext|>
GPT-4 (cl100k_base)~100kBetter digits/whitespace handling; ~fewer tokens per text
Llama 2 (SentencePiece)32,000Small vocab → longer sequences
Llama 3128,2564× bigger vocab bought ~15% shorter sequences

Read that last row as a systems decision, not an ML one: a bigger vocabulary means shorter sequences (cheaper attention, smaller KV cache, more text per context window) at the price of a bigger embedding + unembedding matrix and rarer, worse-trained tail tokens.

5 · Embeddings: meaning becomes geometry

A token ID is arbitrary. The embedding table maps each ID to a learned vector, and word2vec (2013) was the demonstration that shocked everyone: train a tiny model to predict neighboring words, and the vectors arrange themselves so that direction encodes meaning — the famous king − man + woman ≈ queen. Similar words cluster; relationships become directions. (Geometric intuition: 3Blue1Brown's neural-network series.)

The catch: word2vec assigns one static vector per word. "Bank" gets the same vector in "river bank" and "bank run". Meaning depends on context, and a lookup table cannot see context. Holding that thought is the fastest way to understand why the next two sections had to happen.

6 · Seq2seq and the bottleneck

Sutskever et al. 2014 made translation neural: an encoder RNN reads the source sentence and compresses it into one fixed-size vector; a decoder RNN unrolls the translation from that vector, token by token. It worked — and it had an obvious flaw the authors saw immediately: quality collapsed on long sentences. A 60-word sentence and a 6-word sentence both squeeze through the same few-thousand-float bottleneck. Compression loss, made architectural.

Vocabulary Autoregressive decoding — generate one token, feed it back in, generate the next — enters the story here and never leaves. It is why LLM inference is sequential per request, why decode is memory-bound, and why batching exists (Lesson 0001). Seq2seq is the direct ancestor of every chat model you will serve.

7 · Attention: the breakthrough

Bahdanau et al. 2014 removed the bottleneck with one move: keep all encoder states, and at every decoding step let the decoder compute a soft, learned weighted average over them — attending to whichever source words matter right now. No fixed vector; alignment is learned, not annotated. Three years later, "Attention Is All You Need" (2017) asked the radical question — if attention is doing the real work, why keep the RNN at all? — and the Transformer was born: attention layers only, fully parallel across the sequence during training. Contextual embeddings (ELMo, BERT) then fixed word2vec's static-vector problem: the same word now gets a different vector in every sentence.

YearIdeaWhat it fixed
2013word2vecMeaning as geometry — but one static vector per word
2014seq2seqEnd-to-end generation — but a fixed-vector bottleneck
2014Bahdanau attentionDecoder looks back at the whole input, softly
2017TransformerDrop recurrence; attention only; parallel training
2018ELMo / BERT / GPTContextual vectors; pretrain once, adapt everywhere

The attention math itself — Q, K, V, and why the K/V part becomes the cache you will spend Phase 4 managing — is the whole of Lesson p0-09. (Deeper treatment: Stanford CS336, Karpathy's "Let's build GPT".)

8 · Sharp edges: when tokenization bites

Failure modes worth knowing cold, because they surface as production incidents, not ML curiosities (all demonstrated in the tokenizer video):

  • Glitch tokens. Tokens frequent in tokenizer-training data but near-absent from model-training data (the "SolidGoldMagikarp" family) have untrained embeddings — feeding them in produces bizarre outputs. Tokenizer and model were trained on different corpora; the mismatch is the bug.
  • Arithmetic and spelling. "2437" may be one token or three depending on context, and "strawberry" is ~2 tokens — the model never sees letters, which is why letter-counting and digit manipulation are unreasonably hard.
  • Multilingual fertility. An English-heavy tokenizer spends 2–5× more tokens per character on underrepresented languages. Same prompt, same model — more latency, less effective context, bigger bill. Your lab measures this for Georgian.
  • Whitespace and code. Indentation is tokens; cl100k merged runs of spaces specifically to stop Python from wasting the context window.
  • Tokenizer/model mismatch in serving. Deploy a model with the wrong tokenizer files and you get fluent garbage — every ID maps to the wrong string. Classic infra incident; version tokenizers like model weights.

9 · Project step: tokens are the capacity unit

Your spine project's gateway will enforce SLOs in tokens/sec, meter cost per token, and size its KV cache per token. Tokenizer fertility therefore feeds capacity planning directly:

Same 500-char prompt through an English-centric tokenizer:
  English  : 500 chars ÷ ~4.0 chars/token ≈ 125 tokens
  Georgian : 500 chars ÷ ~1.4 chars/token ≈ 357 tokens   (~2.9x)

Consequences per request, same model, same hardware:
  prefill compute  ~2.9x    KV-cache bytes  ~2.9x
  effective context ÷2.9    per-request bill ~2.9x

Interview talking points

  • "BPE is greedy compression: repeatedly merge the most frequent adjacent pair until a vocab budget; encoding just replays the merges in order."
  • "Byte-level BPE has no out-of-vocabulary case — worst case, a string falls back to raw bytes."
  • "Vocab size is a systems trade: bigger vocab → shorter sequences, cheaper attention, smaller KV cache — but a bigger embedding table and worse-trained tail tokens."
  • "Seq2seq's flaw was one fixed vector for the whole input; Bahdanau attention let the decoder softly look back at every encoder state, and the Transformer made that the entire architecture."
  • "word2vec gives one static vector per word; ELMo/BERT made vectors contextual — that's the line between embeddings and representations."
  • "Tokenizer fertility varies by language, so capacity planning in tokens/sec must model the tokens-per-character rate of the actual traffic mix."
