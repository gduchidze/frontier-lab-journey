Notification System + Distributed Scheduler — two designs that share a spine

"Send 100M pushes/SMS/emails a day, reliably" and "run cron across a fleet, exactly once" look like different prompts. Underneath they are the same machine: durable pending work, workers who must not double-fire, and a flaky thing at the end of the pipe.

Mission tie. Eval-pipeline orchestration and batch-job scheduling at AI labs are this exact design wearing different clothes — a cron tier firing eval batches into a fanout of judge workers, with a rate-capped model API playing the flaky provider.
Rep protocol — do this FIRST Before reading further: run a 40-minute solo timed rep on ONE of the two prompts — "Design a notification system (push, SMS, email) for 100M notifications/day" or "Design a distributed job scheduler (cron at scale)." Out loud, alone, 4-step framework from lesson 0009. THEN read this lesson and log every delta (missed component, unspent number, unnamed failure mode) as a learning record.

1 · Two prompts, one lesson

Both prompts reduce to one sentence: reliable async fanout with time semantics. A notification system is "deliver this work item to N endpoints now-ish, survive retries and flaky providers." A scheduler is "deliver this work item to a worker at time T, survive crashes and clock drift." Strip the vocabulary and the organs are identical:

OrganNotification systemDistributed scheduler
Durable pending workPer-channel queuesJob store ordered by fire time
Ownership of one itemQueue visibility / ackLease + fencing token
Honest delivery contractAt-least-once + dedup keyAt-least-once + idempotent handler
The flaky dependencyAPNs / SMS / email providerThe job's own side effects
Safety valveDLQ + circuit breakerMissed-fire catch-up policy

That is why they share a lesson: master one spine, narrate it twice. Interviewers grade the same muscles — durable state, ownership, retries, idempotency — in both.

3 · Estimate — numbers you will spend

100M notifications/day ÷ ~100k s/day ≈ 1.2k/s average
Flash event (breaking news, big sale): ×20 peak ≈ 25k/s for minutes
Channel split: 70M push · 25M email · 5M SMS per day

Provider rate caps (contractual, not yours to change):
  push ~10k/s   email ~2k/s   SMS ~200/s
SMS average = 5M/86,400 ≈ 60/s  → fine
SMS at flash peak = ×20 ≈ 1.2k/s vs 200/s cap
  → queue grows ~1k/s; a 10-min spike ≈ 600k backlog
  → drain time 600k ÷ 200/s = 50 minutes

Storage: 100M/day × ~0.5 KB log row ≈ 50 GB/day → keep 30 days ≈ 1.5 TB

Spend every number: the 25k/s peak means the ingestion tier is stateless-horizontal behind an LB. The SMS cap math proves you need per-channel queues (a 50-minute SMS backlog must not delay push OTPs) and priority lanes inside SMS (OTP jumps the campaign backlog). The 1.5 TB log fits one partitioned store — audit is cheap, so keep it.

4 · High-level notification design

clients / services
   → Ingestion API  (validate, auth, assign event_id)
   → Preference + opt-out filter  (drop before you queue)
   → Template service  (render per-channel body)
   → per-channel queues  [push] [email] [sms — priority lanes]
   → channel workers  (rate-limited to provider caps)
   → provider adapters  (APNs, FCM, Twilio, SES)
   → notification log  (every attempt, every outcome)

Narrate the one non-obvious ordering: filter before the queue — enqueueing a notification the user opted out of wastes queue capacity and risks a compliance incident if the filter is down at dequeue time. And say the sentence that frames the whole deep dive: the provider is always the bottleneck and always flaky. You do not control APNs, Twilio has rate caps and outages, email greylists you. Everything downstream of the queue is designed around that fact.

5 · Deep dive: reliability — at-least-once, honestly

The contract is at-least-once delivery + idempotency; anyone promising exactly-once across a third-party HTTP API is bluffing. The kit:

  • Idempotency / dedup key. Every notification carries an event_id assigned at ingestion. Workers and providers that accept idempotency keys (many do) dedupe on it; your own retry path always does.
  • Retries: exponential backoff + jitter — lesson 0008's retry storm, verbatim. A provider that just failed for 10k messages will receive all 10k retries in the same instant unless jitter spreads them.
  • DLQ. After k attempts, park the message with its error history. A poison message (malformed token, dead device) must not consume retry budget forever.
  • Circuit breaker per provider. When Twilio's error rate spikes, stop hammering — fail fast into the queue, probe with trickle traffic, reopen gradually.
  • Notification log. Append-only record of every attempt and outcome. It answers "did user X get the OTP?" in support tickets and feeds delivery-rate monitoring.

6 · Deep dive: dedup and collapsing

The soft no-duplicate guarantee is implemented as a dedup window: on dequeue, SETNX dedup:{event_id} 1 EX 3600 in Redis — if the key already existed, someone else delivered (or is delivering) this event within the window; drop it. It is a window, not a ledger: after TTL a replayed event could slip through, which is exactly what "soft" means, and you say so.

Two more per-user controls sit at the same choke point:

  • Per-user rate limit (lesson 0018's token bucket, scoped per user per channel): no user gets 40 pushes in a minute no matter what upstream does. This is a product guarantee and a bug firewall.
  • Digest / collapse. "17 people liked your post" instead of 17 pushes. Implemented with a collapse key (user:post:like) and a short buffering delay: hold, count, emit one. Marketing loves it; batteries love it more.

7 · Distributed scheduler — high-level

Second prompt: cron at scale — thousands of recurring and one-shot jobs, fired within seconds of their schedule, across a fleet where any node can die. Anatomy: a job store (definitions + next_fire_at, durable DB), a due-job index ordered by fire time, and scheduler workers that claim and fire due jobs. Three classic shapes for the index:

ShapeHowTrade-off
Timing wheelIn-memory ring of time buckets; hand advances per tickO(1) insert/fire; memory-bound, needs rebuild on restart
Sorted set pollRedis ZADD by fire timestamp; workers loop ZRANGEBYSCORE 0 nowSimple, durable-ish, visible; polling adds up to one interval of lag
Delay queueBroker-native delayed delivery (SQS delay, RabbitMQ TTL+DLX)Least code; capped delays, hard to cancel or reschedule

At scale, partition by time-bucket (e.g. one partition per minute-of-hour, or hash of job_id): each partition is owned by one scheduler at a time, so no single node scans the whole schedule and the "everything fires at 00:00" thundering herd spreads across owners.

8 · Deep dive: scheduler correctness

The interview question inside the question: what stops two schedulers firing the same job?

  • Leader per partition. Each partition has one elected owner, via a lease in a coordination store (etcd/ZooKeeper, or a DB row with expiry). Lease expires → someone else takes over.
  • Fencing tokens (the Phase-2 idea, inline refresher): every lease grant carries a monotonically increasing token. Downstream state — the job-run record, the side-effect store — rejects writes carrying an older token than it has already seen. Why leases alone fail: a leader can stall (GC pause, VM freeze), its lease expires, a new leader fires the job — then the zombie wakes and fires again. The zombie holds a stale token, so the downstream write bounces. Lease = probably one owner; fencing = a dead owner's writes are harmless.
  • Missed-fire catch-up policy. The scheduler was down 10 minutes; a job should have fired 10 times. Per-job policy: fire-all (billing ticks), fire-once-latest (report generation), or skip (cache warmers). Making this configurable is a senior signal — there is no universally right answer.
  • The honest contract: at-least-once firing + idempotent job handlers. Fencing plus idempotency keys gives exactly-once effect; exactly-once firing is not promisable through crashes.
  • Clocks. Workers drift; NTP bounds but doesn't eliminate it. One authority decides "due": the job store's clock, never each worker's watch — a worker 1.5 s fast will steal leases and fire early otherwise (your lab demonstrates exactly this).

9 · Failure modes and monitoring

FailureWrong responseRight response
Provider down 20 minDrop or retry-hammerBackpressure, not amnesia: buffer in the channel queue, breaker open, drain within caps after recovery; age-shed only marketing class
Scheduler leader dies mid-tickAssume the tick happenedNew leader recomputes due jobs from durable next_fire_at; fencing blocks the zombie's late writes
Poison messageInfinite retry loopDLQ after k attempts, alert on DLQ depth, replay tooling
Flash event spikeScale the SMS provider (you can't)Queues absorb; priority lanes protect OTP; pre-agreed burst caps

The two metrics that page a human: end-to-end delivery p99 per channel per priority class (enqueue → provider-accepted) and scheduler fire-lag (actual fire time − scheduled time). Supporting cast: DLQ depth, per-provider error rate, dedup-drop rate (a sudden rise means an upstream retry loop).

10 · Variants and twists

  • Webhooks to third parties — the mirror image: a notification system where you are the flaky provider. So behave like a good one: sign payloads, include an event id so consumers can dedupe, publish your retry-with-backoff policy, give up into a "failed webhooks" dashboard the customer can replay from.
  • Ordering per user. "Password changed" must not arrive before "password change requested." Partition queues by user_id so one user's notifications are serialized on one lane — global ordering is never the requirement; per-key ordering usually is.
  • Quiet hours / timezones. "Don't push at 3 a.m. recipient-local" turns every notification into a scheduled delivery at the next allowed local time — the two designs of this lesson literally merge into one system. Say that sentence in the interview.

AI twist (short). An eval pipeline is this spine verbatim: the scheduler fires nightly eval batches; the fanout tier delivers work items to judge workers; the model API is the rate-capped, flaky provider; fencing stops a stalled orchestrator double-running an eval; the DLQ holds prompts that crash the judge.

11 · Interview talking points

  • Framing: "Both of these are reliable async fanout with time semantics — let me build the shared spine, then specialize."
  • Spending a number: "SMS caps at 200/s and the flash peak needs 1.2k/s — that gap is why the SMS queue is deep, priority-laned, and isolated from push."
  • Honest contract: "I'll promise at-least-once plus idempotency and engineer the duplicates down; exactly-once across Twilio's API is not a promise I can keep."
  • Unprompted failure mode: "The scary one is the stalled leader that wakes after its lease expired — fencing tokens make its late writes bounce."
  • Ops close: "The pager metrics are delivery p99 per class and scheduler fire-lag — not CPU."
