Twitter Timeline / News Feed

Every follower relationship is a promise to deliver. Fanout is where you decide when to keep that promise — at write time, at read time, or a threshold-driven mix of both.

Mission tie. The news feed is the canonical read-vs-write amplification trade-off: precompute on write and you multiply writes by follower count; compute on read and you multiply reads by followee count. Every cache/queue/shard tool from Phase 1 gets spent here on one question — where does the multiplication happen? The celebrity problem — one hot key whose fanout starves everyone else — is the same skew you will later fight as hot-tenant scheduling in LLM-serving gateways.
Rep protocol (do this FIRST) Before reading further: run a 40-minute solo timed design, out loud, on the prompt "design the Twitter home timeline" using the lesson-0009 four-step framework (clarify → estimate → high-level → deep dive). Only then read this lesson and log the deltas — what you missed, what you over-built — as a learning record. Reading first turns a rep into a memory test.

1 · The prompt, and what it actually tests

"Design the Twitter home timeline" (equivalently: news feed, Instagram home, LinkedIn feed). The interviewer is testing four things: (1) do you discover the read-heavy ratio and spend it on precomputation; (2) do you see that fanout-on-write is a deliberate choice to move cost from the read path to the write path, not a free lunch; (3) do you find the celebrity problem yourself — ideally in your own Step-2 arithmetic — and reach the hybrid; (4) do you treat feed staleness as an SLO with a monitorable symptom (queue lag), not an afterthought. A candidate who names "fanout" but cannot do the write-amplification math is pattern-matching; the math is the rep.

Out of scope (say it, write it): search, DMs, media processing, ads and ranking models (a twist returns to both in §10), moderation, auth. The freshness SLO is the load-bearing clarification — it is the number that will later disqualify pure push for celebrities.

3 · Estimate (~5 min): numbers you will spend

Writes:  100M posts/day ÷ ~100k s/day ≈ 1.2k posts/s avg, ×3 peak ≈ 3.5k/s
Reads:   200M DAU × 10 feed opens/day = 2B reads/day ≈ 23k QPS avg, ×3 ≈ 70k peak
Ratio:   2B ÷ 100M = 20:1 request-level (each read renders ~20 posts → ~400:1 item-level)
Fanout:  100M posts × 200 avg followers = 20B feed inserts/day
         ≈ 230k inserts/s avg, ×3 ≈ 700k inserts/s peak  ← 200× write amplification
Celebrity: 1 post × 50M followers = 50M inserts ≈ 217 s of the ENTIRE avg fanout capacity
Storage: 100M posts/day × 1 KB ≈ 100 GB/day ≈ 36 TB/year (text only)

Spend every number: 70k read QPS at p99 < 200 ms → reads must be precomputed list lookups, not queries — a feed cache. 200× write amplification → fanout must be asynchronous, behind a queue, horizontally scaled workers; never inline with POST /posts. 50M inserts ≈ 217 s of the whole cluster vs a 60 s freshness SLO → pure fanout-on-write is arithmetically disqualified; hybrid required (§6). 36 TB/year → shard the posts store; time-sortable Snowflake IDs from lesson 0019 become the sort key for free (§8).

4 · High-level design (~15 min): boxes, API, data model

POST /posts                        {text}              → 201 {post_id}
POST /users/{id}/follow            {followee_id}
GET  /feed?cursor={max_id}&limit=20                   → newest-first page
GET  /users/{id}/posts                                 → user (profile) timeline

Data model. posts(post_id SNOWFLAKE PK, author_id, text, created_at), sharded by post_id. follows(follower_id, followee_id) with two indexes: by followee_id (fanout asks "who follows this author?") and by follower_id (read path asks "whom do I follow?"). Feed cache: one Redis list per user, feed:{user_id}, holding post IDs only, capped at ~800 entries.

Write path: client → LB → post service → (a) append to posts store, (b) enqueue a fanout job. Fanout workers pull the job, fetch the author's follower list, and push the post_id onto each follower's Redis list. Read path: feed service reads the user's Redis list, hydrates IDs from a post cache (fall through to DB), merges in celebrity posts at read time (§6), optionally ranks (§8), returns a page. Narrate the trade-off as you draw: the queue buys a fast, durable POST at the price of bounded staleness — which the 60 s SLO explicitly permits.

5 · Deep dive: fanout-on-write vs fanout-on-read

DimensionFanout-on-write (push)Fanout-on-read (pull)
Write amplification200× avg — one insert per follower1× — store the post once
Read latencyO(1): one list fetch + hydratek-way merge over ~200 followee timelines, every read
StorageDuplicated post IDs — terabytes of feed cache (§7)Nothing beyond per-author timelines
StalenessBounded by queue drain — seconds normally, minutes under lagZero — always current at read time
Inactive-user wasteFull fanout work for the ~75% who won't log in this weekZero work for dormant users
FitsNormal accounts in a 20:1 read-heavy systemCelebrities, dormant users, very-low-read systems

The ratio decides the default: at 20:1 request-level (and ~400:1 item-level), paying 200× once per post to make 70k QPS of reads O(1) is a good trade — except where follower count makes "once per post" explode. That exception is the next section, and finding it yourself is the senior signal.

6 · Deep dive: the celebrity problem and the hybrid

Do the arithmetic for one Lady-Gaga-class post, 50M followers. At 10k inserts/s per worker, that is 5,000 worker-seconds — 100 workers grinding ~50 s on one post. Cluster-wide at 700k inserts/s peak capacity it still monopolizes everything for ~71 s — while every normal post queues behind it (head-of-line blocking) and the 60 s freshness SLO burns for the whole site. Worse, those 50M inserts are near-random writes sprayed across every feed-cache shard simultaneously. A handful of accounts can generate more fanout load than the other 200M combined. Pure push does not degrade gracefully under skew — it fails at its heaviest key.

The hybrid: pick a follower-count threshold (set it where fanout time for one post ≈ your freshness SLO). Below it, push as normal. Above it, don't fan out at all: the celebrity's post stays only on their author timeline. The read path becomes: fetch the user's precomputed list, plus the recent author timelines of the few celebrities they follow (usually < 10), and k-way merge by ID. The cost moves to reads — but a celebrity's author timeline is the hottest, most cacheable data in the system: one cached copy serves millions of merges. Dormant users get the same treatment for the opposite reason: no push for accounts unlikely to read (§7 eviction), rebuild by pull when they return.

7 · Deep dive: feed cache internals

Per user: LPUSH feed:{uid} post_id then LTRIM feed:{uid} 0 799 — an O(1) capped insert. (A sorted set keyed by Snowflake ID adds dedup and range queries at higher memory cost; the list is the cheap default.) 800 entries ≈ 40 pages of scroll — beyond realistic depth; deeper history is rebuildable from the posts store, so the cap is safe.

IDs alone:  200M users × 800 entries × 8 B (Snowflake) ≈ 1.28 TB
Redis overhead (list nodes, pointers, key space): ×3–5 → ~4–6 TB
→ a sharded cluster of dozens–hundreds of nodes … unless you evict

Eviction is a design decision, not an ops detail. Only materialize feeds for recently active users (LRU / ~30-day TTL). If ~25% of users are weekly-active, resident feeds drop to ~50M → ~320 GB of IDs (~1–1.5 TB real) — from "hundreds of nodes" to "a modest cluster". A returning user's first read misses, falls back to fanout-on-read to rebuild the list, then resumes push. This is exactly the inactive-user-waste row of §5's table turned into architecture: don't push to people who don't read.

8 · Deep dive: ordering, ranking, pagination

Ordering. Snowflake IDs (lesson 0019) are time-sortable, so "merge by time" is "merge by ID" — no timestamp joins. The read path's k-way merge (own list + celebrity timelines) is a heap-merge over sorted-by-ID lists: cheap, and identical in shape to the merge every LSM-tree and search engine does.

Pagination. Cursor, not offset: the client passes the smallest post_id it has seen (max_id), the server returns the next 20 older items. Offsets break in a feed because new posts arrive continuously — page 2 by offset shifts under the reader and shows duplicates. The cursor is stable because IDs only grow.

Ranking. The ranking service is an insertion point, not a redesign: fetch ~800 chronological candidates exactly as above, score them (recency, author affinity, engagement), rerank, return. Candidates-then-rank keeps the storage layer chronological and makes "Latest" mode a one-flag fallback — which is also your degraded mode when the ranker is down.

9 · Failure modes & monitoring

FailureSymptomMitigation
Fanout queue lag growsFeeds go silently stale — no errors anywhere; posts vanish for followers until the queue drainsAlert on queue age, not depth; end-to-end freshness probe (synthetic post → time-to-visible); autoscale workers
Feed-cache shard diesAffected users' feeds empty; mass rebuild-by-pull stampedeReplica per shard; coalesce rebuilds (one flight per user); jittered backoff (lesson 0008)
Thundering herd on login after an outageCold caches × login spike → pull-path overload on the posts storeServe degraded feed (sample of followees), request coalescing, gradual cache warm-up

Dashboard: fanout queue age & insert rate, feed read p99, freshness-probe latency, Redis memory/evictions per shard. The metric that pages a human is the freshness probe — queue lag is its leading indicator.

10 · Variants & twists

  • Ads / injected content. Never fan out ads — targeting is per-viewer, so injection belongs at read/rank time, budgeted per page. Same slot mechanism serves "suggested posts".
  • Notify vs pull-to-refresh. Instead of pushing content, push a cheap counter ("12 new posts") and deliver content on the user's explicit refresh — halves effective fanout for lurkers and turns delivery into demand-driven work.
  • Multi-datacenter. Home-region the user: feed cache lives near the reader; posts replicate globally; fanout runs per region into local caches (write amplification × #regions) — versus single-home, which is cheaper but adds cross-region latency for travelers. The freshness SLO now includes replication lag.

AI corner. An LLM gateway with one hot tenant is this exact lesson wearing GPUs: a whale's burst can head-of-line-block the shared batch queue for everyone, and the medicine is identical — per-tenant queues, a threshold that reclassifies whales, and special-cased scheduling for them instead of pretending all keys are equal.

11 · Interview talking points

  • Spending the ratio: "20:1 reads and a 60-second freshness budget — that pair is why I precompute feeds on write instead of querying on read."
  • Finding the dragon yourself: "Before you ask: 50M followers × 1 post is 50M inserts — about 217 seconds of my entire fanout capacity, versus a 60-second SLO. Pure push is arithmetically dead; here's the hybrid."
  • Threshold justification: "The celebrity cutoff isn't folklore — it's where fanout time for one post crosses the freshness SLO."
  • Memory math on demand: "200M users × 800 IDs × 8 bytes is 1.28 TB before Redis overhead — which is why I only materialize feeds for active users and rebuild the rest by pull."
  • Failure mode unprompted: "The scariest failure is silent: queue lag means stale feeds with zero errors. I monitor freshness end-to-end with a synthetic-post probe, not just queue depth."
  • Pagination detail: "Cursor by max_id, because offsets shift as new posts land — and Snowflake IDs give me the time-ordering for free."
