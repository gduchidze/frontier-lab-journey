Monolith → Microservices

Decomposition is a trade you pay for in latency, failure math, and ops. This lesson prices the trade — in-process calls vs network hops, 0.99920, tail amplification — so you know when to split, how to split, and above all when NOT to.

Mission tie. Anthropic's design prompts hand you a system and watch which boxes you draw — and weak candidates draw twelve microservices by reflex. Strong candidates start from one process, name the specific force (scaling, team autonomy, fault isolation) that justifies each cut, and then price the cut: "twenty services in a serial chain at three nines each is a 98% system." Interviewers escalate on exactly this arithmetic, because it separates architecture from box-drawing. This lesson gives you the forces, the criteria, and the numbers. (Exponent's 2026 Anthropic guide)

1 · The monolith is the right default

One codebase, one process, one deploy. Before scorning it, list what you get for free: a function call between modules costs ~50 nanoseconds; a network hop between services costs ~1 millisecond — a 20,000× cliff you re-cross on every decomposed call. Transactions spanning modules are a single ACID COMMIT. Debugging is one stack trace. Deploys, rollbacks, and local dev are one artifact. DHH calls this the "majestic monolith" — Basecamp serves millions from one, on purpose. Fowler's advice is blunter: MonolithFirst — nearly every successful microservice story he has seen started with a monolith that grew too big; systems built as microservices from scratch tend to end in serious trouble, because you only learn the right service boundaries by operating the domain.

The strongest counterexample to "big must mean microservices" is Shopify: one of the world's largest Rails codebases, deliberately kept as a modular monolith — code split into components with enforced internal boundaries (their packwerk tooling fails the build on illegal cross-component calls), while deployment stays one unit. Boundaries without the network. That is the position to defend by default in an interview: modularity is mandatory, distribution is optional.

2 · What actually forces the split

Four forces — and note that most are organizational, not technical:

  • Independent scaling. Checkout needs 50 machines at peak; invoicing needs 2. In a monolith you scale the whole binary to the hottest module's needs and waste the difference.
  • Team autonomy (Conway's law). "Organizations design systems that mirror their communication structures." Fifty engineers in one codebase serialize on merge queues, shared schemas, and release trains. Amazon hit this wall around 2001: Werner Vogels describes carving the retail monolith into services, each owned end-to-end — "you build it, you run it" — by a two-pizza team, precisely so teams could deploy without asking permission (ACM Queue: A Conversation with Werner Vogels).
  • Fault isolation. A memory leak in the recommendations module takes down checkout when they share a process. As separate services, recommendations can die while checkout degrades gracefully to "no suggestions today."
  • Independent deploy cadence. The pricing team ships hourly; the ledger team ships weekly after audit. One deploy pipeline forces both to the slower, scarier rhythm.

Interview move: never justify a split with "scalability" in the abstract. Name which of these four forces applies to this system, or keep the module in-process.

3 · Where to cut: bounded contexts, not layers

Given a real force, where do the seams go? By business capability, never by technical layer. A "UI service + business-logic service + database service" split means every user action crosses all of them — you have distributed your layers, not your domain. Cut instead along DDD bounded contexts: Orders, Payments, Inventory, Shipping — units where a word like "customer" has one consistent meaning, and which change for one reason. Fowler and Lewis's original microservices article makes this the first characteristic: services organized around business capabilities, each owning its own data, replaceable independently.

And you migrate incrementally, never big-bang: the strangler fig. Put a routing facade in front of the monolith, carve out one capability at a time behind it, shift traffic route by route, and let the monolith shrink until what remains is deletable. The old system keeps serving production throughout — every step is small, reversible, and shippable, which is exactly what a two-year rewrite is not.

4 · The microservice tax — the arithmetic interviewers want to hear

Every split re-prices three things. Say these numbers unprompted and you sound like you have operated this.

Latency. The in-process call you replaced was free; the network hop has a mean (~1 ms in-datacenter) and a tail. In the lab, the same 20 ms of work split across a serial chain: p99 goes from 20 ms (monolith) to ~29 ms at N=2, ~44 ms at N=10, ~58 ms at N=20. The mean grew 1 ms per hop; the p99 grew almost twice as fast, because tails add across serial hops.

Availability. A serial chain multiplies failure. Each service at 99.9%: 0.99920 ≈ 98.0% — your twenty three-nines services compose into a two-nines system. At 10 M requests/day that is ~200,000 failures/day, versus ~10,000 for the single-service baseline. Retries claw nines back but multiply load — and a retry storm during a partial outage is extra traffic aimed precisely at the sick service (AWS Builders' Library: timeouts, retries, and backoff with jitter).

Tail amplification under fan-out. When a page calls N services in parallel, it waits for the slowest. If each backend exceeds its p99 1% of the time, the chance your request hits at least one straggler is 1 − 0.99N: 18% at N=20, 63% at N=100 — the rare slow call becomes the common case. This is the central result of Dean & Barroso's "The Tail at Scale"; their mitigation, hedged requests (send a backup copy after the 95th-percentile delay, take the first answer), caps the tail for ~5% extra load — you will measure exactly this trade in the lab.

Then the bill that never fits in a table: N deploy pipelines, N on-call rotations, distributed tracing to reassemble one request's story, service discovery, versioned APIs between everything. The tax is real; pay it only where §2's forces earn it back.

5 · Service discovery — how services find each other

Once instances scale up, die, and move, hardcoded addresses are over. Instances register (or are registered) in a service registry — Consul, etcd, ZooKeeper — with health checks (heartbeats or TTLs) so dead instances stop resolving. Two lookup topologies (Primer §Service discovery):

ModelWho queries the registryWinsPaysExamples
Client-side (pattern)The calling service itself, then load-balances across instancesOne less hop; client picks smart per-request choicesDiscovery logic in every client, per languageNetflix Eureka + Ribbon

Transport matters too: DNS-based discovery (Consul DNS, K8s cluster DNS) is universal but caches — TTLs delay failover; API-based discovery (registry queries, or a watch stream) is fresh but couples you to the registry client. Modern platforms mostly hide this: a Kubernetes Service name is server-side discovery with health-checked endpoints behind stable DNS. In an interview, one sentence suffices — "instances register in Consul with TTL health checks; callers resolve through the mesh/LB" — but know which model you just described.

6 · API gateway vs BFF

Clients should not know your internal topology. An API gateway (pattern) is the single front door that handles edge concerns once: authentication, rate limiting, TLS termination, routing, request aggregation — so thirty services don't implement auth thirty ways. The costs are symmetrical: it is a chokepoint that can become a single point of failure (so it runs replicated and dumb), and a shared codebase every team wants to edit (so it stays thin — routing and policy, not business logic).

When one general-purpose gateway starts accumulating per-client aggregation — mobile wants a lean payload, web wants a rich one, TV wants something else — Sam Newman's Backend-for-Frontend pattern splits the edge: one small backend per frontend, owned by that frontend's team, each shaping and aggregating for exactly one client. You trade some duplication for autonomy — which is the whole microservices trade in miniature, replayed at the edge.

7 · Data ownership — the join you lost

The rule that makes services real: database-per-service (pattern). Each service owns its data; nobody else touches its tables. Shared tables are a shared schema, and a shared schema is a deploy-time coupling that silently reassembles the monolith. The price is steep and specific: the join is gone. "Orders joined with customer names" was one SQL line; now it is either an API composition (call both, join in memory — fan-out tax applies) or a materialized local copy — the Orders service keeps a read-only projection of the customer fields it needs, updated by events. That copy is eventually consistent: for some window, Orders may show a customer's old name. Cross-service transactions are likewise gone; sagas and event-driven consistency get their own treatment in lessons 0013–0014 — for now, know that every arrow between two services on your whiteboard is also a consistency boundary (DDIA, chs. 7 and 9, is the deep background).

8 · Failure modes that decide interviews

  • The distributed monolith — the worst of both. Services that deploy in lockstep, share a database, and call each other synchronously for everything: you pay every network and ops tax of microservices while keeping every coupling of the monolith. Diagnostic questions: can you deploy one service alone? Does one schema change fan out to five repos? Does every request thread through the same call chain?
  • Cascading failure via synchronous chains. A → B → C, C slows, B's threads block waiting, B's pool exhausts, A follows — one slow leaf takes the whole chain down, and retries amplify the wave. Defenses: timeouts, circuit breakers, bounded queues, load shedding (Google SRE book: Addressing Cascading Failures). Deeper treatment next lessons; name the mechanism now.
  • Version skew. With N services deploying independently, both old and new API versions are always live somewhere mid-rollout. Contracts must be backward-compatible (add fields, never repurpose them), and consumer-driven contract tests replace the compiler you lost when the call left the process.
  • Org smaller than architecture. Fowler's MicroservicePrerequisites — "you must be this tall": rapid provisioning, basic monitoring, rapid deployment, DevOps culture. Five engineers running forty services fail this bar. Segment famously reversed course: they consolidated ~140 per-destination services back into a monolith after the operational overhead — versioning drift across dozens of repos, per-service queues and autoscaling — swamped a small team. Consolidation restored their velocity. Merging back is not failure; it is re-pricing the trade.

9 · One AI note, then back to fundamentals

The spine project maps cleanly onto this lesson: an LLM inference platform decomposes into gateway → router → batching scheduler → workers → KV-cache manager because §2's forces are genuinely present — GPU workers scale on different hardware than the CPU-bound gateway, and the scheduler deploys far more often than workers holding model weights. But the tax still rules the cuts: the tokenizer stays in-process with the gateway or worker, because tokenizing is a sub-millisecond CPU task and a "tokenizer service" would spend more on the network hop than on the work — the purest microservice-tax violation there is. Same forces, same arithmetic; that is the whole note.

10 · Interview talking points

How to deploy this lesson live, in order:

  1. Start monolith-first, out loud. "I'd begin as a modular monolith — Shopify-style enforced module boundaries — and split only where a force demands it." This instantly separates you from reflexive box-drawers.
  2. Name the force per split (§2): "checkout splits for independent scaling; payments splits for fault and compliance isolation" — never a bare "for scalability."
  3. Cut by capability, not layer (§3), and migrate strangler-fig: facade in front, one capability at a time, reversible steps.
  4. Price the tax unprompted (§4): "20 services serial at three nines is 98% end-to-end — so I'll keep the synchronous chain to 3–4 services and make the rest async."
  5. Raise the tail: "the page fans out to 20 backends, so 18% of requests hit a p99 straggler — I'd hedge requests at p95 for ~5% extra load." Citing Tail at Scale by name is a strong signal.
  6. State the data rule (§7): database-per-service, joins become composition or materialized views, eventual consistency named with a bound.
  7. Volunteer the failure modes (§8): distributed monolith, cascade via sync chains ("timeouts + circuit breakers + shedding"), and the org bar — mention Segment's reversal to show you know the trade runs both ways.

12 · Project step

Decision time for the LLM inference platform: which components are separate services, and which live in one process? Defend it in two sentences. A defensible pick: gateway, router/scheduler, and GPU workers are separate services because they scale on different hardware and deploy on different cadences; the tokenizer and per-worker KV-cache bookkeeping stay in-process with the worker, because a ~0.1 ms CPU task must not pay a ~1 ms network hop on every request. Write your own two sentences — name the force for every cut, name the tax for every merge — and bring them to the next session for critique.
